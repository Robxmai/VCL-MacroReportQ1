# South African Consumer Psychology: A Comparative Analysis (2023-2026)

## Executive Summary

South African consumer psychology has undergone significant transformation between 2023 and 2026, shaped by a complex interplay of macroeconomic forces, infrastructural challenges, and evolving societal values. The period witnessed a dramatic trajectory in consumer confidence, which reached its lowest point at -20 in Q1 2025 following a "tax shock" before recovering to -7 by Q1 2026, representing the highest reading since Q4 2024[1][2]. Inflation has moderated substantially from 6.08% in 2023 to 3.0% in February 2026, providing meaningful relief to household purchasing power[7][8]. However, this apparent improvement masks deeply entrenched behavioral shifts, as consumers have fundamentally restructured their financial approaches around defensive consumption patterns. The proliferation of digital commerce accelerated dramatically, with the e-commerce market reaching R225 billion in 2025, growing at 15% annually and capturing 16% of total retail, triple the pace of traditional retail[11]. Most notably, South African consumers have developed a bifurcated psychology—expressed optimism about the future coexists with active financial restructuring, with 72% feeling positive about their financial outlook while 51% have cut discretionary spending and 36% expect to miss loan repayments[4][15]. The fundamental transformation in consumer behavior reflects not merely cyclical adjustments but structural changes in how South Africans conceptualize value, allocate resources, and navigate economic uncertainty.

## 1. Introduction

Understanding consumer psychology is essential for businesses, policymakers, and economists seeking to navigate South Africa's complex retail landscape. This report provides a comprehensive comparative analysis of South African consumer psychology between 2023 and 2026, examining how external factors have shaped attitudinal and behavioral changes across multiple dimensions. The analysis draws upon extensive primary research from the Bureau for Economic Research at Stellenbosch University, TransUnion's quarterly Consumer Pulse studies, NielsenIQ retail data, Statistics South Africa, and numerous other authoritative sources to construct a nuanced picture of how South African consumers have evolved during this period.

The timeframe under examination represents one of the most challenging periods for South African consumers in recent history, characterized by the lingering aftermath of global inflationary pressures, significant policy shifts including VAT increases, persistent infrastructure challenges in the form of loadshedding, and volatile fuel prices. Yet this period also witnessed meaningful improvements in certain macroeconomic indicators, creating an environment of uneven recovery and divergent consumer experiences depending on income level, geographic location, and demographic profile.

The report examines six primary dimensions of consumer psychology: economic sentiment, spending behaviors, digital adoption, values and priorities, life stage and demographic changes, and external factor impacts. Each section traces the evolution from 2023 to 2026 where data permits, identifies the key factors that drove observed changes, and considers implications for the future. The analysis recognizes that South African consumers represent a heterogeneous population with vastly different experiences of economic conditions, and wherever possible, disaggregates findings by income level, generation, and other relevant demographic characteristics.

## 2. Economic Sentiment Changes

### 2.1 Consumer Confidence Trajectory

The trajectory of South African consumer confidence between 2023 and 2026 reveals a volatile path shaped by successive shocks and subsequent recovery. The FNB/BER Consumer Confidence Index, which measures net consumer sentiment by asking about expected economic performance, household financial position, and appropriateness of present time to buy durable goods, provides the most comprehensive longitudinal view of consumer psychology during this period[1].

In 2023, consumer confidence was mired in negative territory, reflecting the accumulated weight of economic pressures from the preceding years. The index averaged approximately -15 to -12 throughout 2024, with a notable improvement in Q3 2024 when the index reached -5, its highest level since the first half of 2019[1]. This improvement reflected growing optimism about inflation moderation, expectations of interest rate cuts, and preliminary signs of loadshedding abatement. However, Q1 2025 delivered a severe shock to consumer sentiment, with the index plummeting to -20, its lowest reading in the survey's recent history. This dramatic deterioration was attributed to what analysts characterized as a "tax shock," referring to the VAT increase and other fiscal measures introduced in the 2025 budget that directly impacted household purchasing power[1].

The subsequent recovery was remarkably swift. By Q2 2025, the index had rebounded to -10, and it continued improving through Q4 2025 to reach -9, before stabilizing at -7 in Q1 2026[1][2]. This recovery was driven by a confluence of positive factors including the repo rate cut to 6.75% in November 2025, stronger-than-expected retail performance during the festive season, and improving financial conditions for higher-income households. According to Trading Economics data, the Q1 2026 reading of -7 represents the highest level since Q4 2024, though it remains below the historical average of -2.41 points measured from 1982 to 2026[2].

The divergent experiences between income groups represent a critical feature of the 2023-2026 period. High-income households demonstrated substantially more confidence throughout, with buoyant asset prices, particularly in equities and property, bolstering household wealth and spending capacity[2]. Lower-income groups, by contrast, remained more pessimistic, weighed down by weak employment growth in late 2025 and stricter compliance requirements for social grants that reduced accessible support for the most vulnerable consumers[2]. This income-divergent recovery has significant implications for the composition of consumer spending and the types of products and services driving retail growth.

### 2.2 Inflation Impact on Purchasing Decisions

The moderation of inflation from 6.08% in 2023 to 3.0% in February 2026 represents one of the most significant developments in the South African macroeconomic landscape during this period[8][9]. According to OECD Economic Surveys data, inflation peaked at 5.9% in 2024 before declining to 4.4% in 2025, with the forecast for 2026 at 3.2%—below the 4.5% target rate[9]. This disinflationary trend has meaningfully improved real purchasing power, though the distribution of benefits has been uneven.

The impact of falling inflation on purchasing decisions operates through multiple channels. Most directly, lower inflation reduces the pace at which prices increase, allowing consumers to purchase more with the same nominal income. However, the relationship between inflation perception and purchasing behavior is more complex than simple price-level effects. Research from NIQ indicates that despite meaningful inflation improvements, consumer price sensitivity has remained elevated, with shoppers continuing to hunt for bargains and switch between brands and stores to maximize value[14]. The persistence of this behavior reflects learned habits from the high-inflation period rather than current conditions.

Food inflation, which disproportionately affects lower-income households who spend a larger share of income on essentials, showed particularly notable improvement. Statistics South Africa reported that annual consumer price inflation was just 2.8% in April 2025, providing meaningful relief for households struggling with elevated food costs[7]. However, this improvement was partially offset by continued increases in administered prices, particularly electricity and water, which have outpaced general inflation significantly since 2020[4].

The phenomenon of "shrinkflation"—where manufacturers reduce product sizes while maintaining or increasing prices—became increasingly salient during this period. NIQ research found that this tactic was becoming less effective as consumers pushed back, with shoppers increasingly sensitive to value changes and willing to switch brands orretailers when they detected erosion in value proposition[14]. This consumer resistance has forced manufacturers to reconsider pricing strategies and has contributed to the growth of private label offerings, which increased their market share significantly during 2024 and 2025.

### 2.3 Interest Rate Sensitivity

Interest rate dynamics underwent significant evolution between 2023 and 2026, with the South African Reserve Bank's monetary policy stance shifting from tightening to easing. The repo rate, which stood at 8.25% in late 2023, was gradually reduced through 2024 and 2025, reaching 6.75% following the November 2025 cut—the first reduction in the easing cycle[5][6]. This 150 basis point reduction over approximately two years represented a meaningful shift in borrowing costs and has had substantial implications for consumer behavior.

The prime lending rate declined to 10.25% following the November 2025 cut, reducing monthly payments for consumers with variable-rate credit facilities including home mortgages, vehicle financing, and personal loans[5]. For a typical R1 million home loan, this rate reduction translates to savings of several hundred rand per month, which while modest in absolute terms, represents meaningful relief for cash-strapped households.

The impact of interest rate changes on consumer psychology operates through both wealth effects and cash flow effects. Lower interest rates support asset prices, particularly residential property, which improved household balance sheets and bolstered consumer confidence among homeowners[19]. Simultaneously, reduced debt service costs free up disposable income for other spending categories, though many consumers have chosen to direct these savings toward accelerated debt repayment rather than increased consumption[15].

TransUnion research revealed that 91% of South Africans view credit as important for achieving financial goals, while 33% identified high borrowing costs as the primary barrier to credit access[15]. The easing of interest rates has improved credit affordability and contributed to growth in certain credit categories, with credit card lending growing at 8.0% and personal loans expanding at 10.2% by Q4 2025[16]. However, lending standards remained relatively tight, with 44% of consumers who considered applying for credit choosing not to proceed, citing fear of rejection and affordability concerns[15].

### 2.4 Price Sensitivity Escalation

Price sensitivity has escalated dramatically during the 2023-2026 period, transforming from a background consideration into a primary driver of consumer behavior. Multiple research sources confirm this shift, with NIQ identifying affordability and low pricing as the single biggest reason for brand choice among South African shoppers[14]. GeoScope data shows that brand loyalty to staple categories weakened significantly by the end of 2023 as consumers became more willing to switch for price advantage[10].

The expression of price sensitivity has evolved beyond simple purchase decisions to encompass fundamental changes in shopping behavior. Consumers are increasingly splitting baskets across multiple retailers to access different promotions and maximize value, a behavior that increases shopping frequency but reduces average transaction size[14]. Promotions and loyalty programs have become more important than ever, with consumers willing to travel further and invest more time in shopping to secure better prices.

This heightened price sensitivity reflects both cyclical and structural factors. Cyclically, the elevated inflation period of 2022-2024 eroded purchasing power and taught consumers to prioritize essentials and hunt for bargains. Structurally, the concentration of retail ownership in South Africa, with major players dominating the market, has limited consumer choice and intensified competition on price as the primary battleground. Discount retailers including the Boxer chain reported 12% sales increases and 16% trading profit growth during 2024, significantly outpacing mainstream retail formats[21].

The distributional impact of heightened price sensitivity is notable. Higher-income consumers have maintained spending while becoming more discerning, prioritizing quality and value over simply purchasing the cheapest option. Lower-income consumers, by contrast, have been forced into more defensive consumption patterns, prioritizing essential categories and trading down wherever possible[4]. This bifurcation has created a two-track consumer market where premium and value segments perform well while middle-market brands and retailers face intense pressure.

## 3. Spending Behavior Evolution

### 3.1 Discretionary vs Essential Spending Shifts

The reallocation of spending from discretionary to essential categories represents one of the most significant behavioral shifts observed between 2023 and 2026. Daily Maverick research describing South Africans as "shifting into survival mode" captures the fundamental nature of this transformation[4]. Rather than simply spending less, consumers have actively restructured their financial behavior around essential costs, with discretionary categories bearing the brunt of cutbacks.

Electricity costs exemplify the essential burden facing South African households. Since 2020, electricity costs have increased approximately 85%, far outpacing general inflation of approximately 30%[4]. Water costs have similarly surged 68%, while public school fees and healthcare costs have also exceeded general inflation. These administered and essential costs now consume substantially larger shares of household budgets, particularly for lower-income families where essentials account for roughly two-thirds of total spending[4]. The consequence is that discretionary categories—from dining out to apparel to entertainment—have become squeezed even when headline inflation improves.

Entertainment spending illustrates this discretionary contraction vividly. DStv subscription data reveals that premium and mid-tier packages contracted sharply, declining 67% and 53% respectively since 2021, while entry-level packages surged by 139%[10]. This migration toward affordable entertainment options reflects consumers' determination to maintain some recreational spending while drastically reducing its cost. Eating out has similarly declined, with weekly dining occasions down nearly 30% compared to pre-crisis levels[10].

Grocery basket composition provides another window into essential-versus-discretionary shifts. The nominal monthly grocery spend rose from R2,050 in 2023 to just over R2,100 in 2024—barely keeping pace with inflation and indicating that real volumes declined[10]. Consumers paid more for less, substituting downwards, buying smaller quantities, and prioritizing staples over premium or discretionary food items. Apparel spending contracted across men's, women's, and children's clothing in real terms, with the apparel sector experiencing sustained pressure throughout 2024 and into 2025[10].

The TransUnion Q4 2025 Consumer Pulse Study quantified these shifts with precision: 50% of South Africans have reduced discretionary spending on dining out, entertainment, and travel; 34% have cancelled subscriptions or memberships; while simultaneously 35% are accelerating debt repayments and 27% are setting aside more in emergency funds[15]. This combination of reduced discretionary spending and increased financial prudence characterizes the defensive consumption mindset that has become prevalent.

### 3.2 Trading Down Behaviors

Trading down—switching from premium to more affordable alternatives within categories—has emerged as a defining behavioral pattern during the 2023-2026 period. This phenomenon extends beyond simple price sensitivity to encompass fundamental reassessments of value propositions across virtually every consumption category.

The grocery sector has witnessed substantial trading down activity. Consumers have shifted toward private label offerings, with discount retailers gaining market share at the expense of premium grocers. The NielsenIQ Retail Spend Barometer documented this shift, noting that FMCG value growth dropped from 7.2% in Q2 2023 to 4.8% in Q2 2024, reflecting both volume declines and mix shifts toward lower-priced formats and brands[14]. Boxer, the discount retail chain, reported 12% sales increases and 16% trading profit growth during 2024, demonstrating the appetite for value formats[21].

Premium food and beverage brands have experienced particular pressure as consumers trade down within categories. Entry-level alcohol options have gained share as consumers reduce spending on premium spirits and wines. Basic household and personal care products have similarly gained at the expense of premium offerings, with consumers accepting lower functionality or brand prestige in exchange for better prices.

The entertainment sector shows perhaps the most dramatic trading down. Netflix subscription tiers have shifted toward lower-cost options, while the DStv migration toward entry-level packages represents a mass market trading down of unprecedented scale[10]. The growth of free-to-air alternatives, streaming piracy, and social entertainment has further accelerated as consumers seek to maintain entertainment access while dramatically reducing expenditure.

Brand loyalty data from GeoScope reveals the complexity of trading down behavior. While brand loyalty to staple categories weakened by the end of 2023 as consumers switched for price advantage, by 2024 the trend had partially reversed, with more consumers reporting they were "always buying the same brands"[10]. This apparent contradiction reflects growing price-driven loyalty to value-oriented brands rather than exploration of premium alternatives. Consumers are remaining loyal to brands that deliver acceptable quality at affordable prices, having abandoned premium options but not devoting substantial effort to ongoing brand search.

### 3.3 Premium vs Value Brand Preferences

The premium versus value preference dynamic has shifted substantially during the period under analysis, with value brands gaining meaningful share while premium brands face intensified pressure except among higher-income consumers. This bifurcation reflects the widening gap between consumer segments and has significant implications for brand strategy and retail positioning.

Research from BMR indicates that South Africans are not simply buying the cheapest option but rather "buying into what brands stand for," suggesting that value preference does not imply abandonment of brand considerations[18]. Rather, consumers seek brands that deliver authentic value propositions aligned with their financial circumstances and personal values. This has created opportunities for brands that can credibly offer quality at accessible price points.

Private label growth exemplifies the value shift. Takealot Group's private label offerings have gained share, while retailers across formats have expanded their value ranges. The average grocery basket shrinkage combined with nominal spend increases indicates consumers are accepting smaller pack sizes and less-premium brands rather than abandoning retail altogether[10]. For many categories, consumers have found acceptable substitutes at lower price points, though certain "hero" brands with strong equity retain premium pricing power.

Among higher-income consumers, particularly those benefiting from improved asset prices and lower interest rates, premium spending has remained more resilient. The property market surge of 17% in rand value during 2025 created wealth effects that supported consumer confidence and discretionary spending among upper-income brackets[19]. These consumers have maintained preferences for quality brands and are willing to pay for premium products, though they too have become more discerning and promotional-responsive.

The cosmetics and personal care sector illustrates the nuanced dynamics. GeoScope data shows cosmetics purchasing remained fairly stable while groceries and toiletries showed weakening loyalty[10]. This suggests that for categories perceived as more personal or where brand differentiation is valued, consumers may maintain premium preferences even while trading down in everyday essentials.

### 3.4 Bulk Buying vs Frequent Small Purchases

The shift away from bulk buying toward more frequent small purchases represents a significant change in shopping patterns during this period. Research consistently indicates that rather than stockpiling or buying in larger quantities to secure savings, South African consumers have been reducing average transaction sizes and shopping more frequently to manage cash flow and minimize waste[10].

The average grocery basket has continued to shrink according to GeoScope data, with consumers buying smaller quantities on each shopping trip[10]. This behavior reflects multiple pressures: constrained cash flow limiting ability to make large upfront purchases, uncertainty about future price levels encouraging immediate rather than forward purchasing, and reduced storage capacity among consumers living in smaller dwellings or managing tight budgets.

Small format retail has benefited from this trend. Convenience stores, spaza shops, and discount retailers offering smaller pack sizes have gained share at the expense of bulk formats and large hypermarkets. The growth of quick-commerce platforms offering rapid delivery of smaller orders has also accelerated, with Checkers Sixty60 reporting 1.5 million orders weekly[11]. This convenience-oriented shopping pattern, while operationally efficient for retailers, may increase per-unit costs for consumers who sacrifice volume discounts.

Communal financial mechanisms provide a partial exception to the small-purchase trend. Stokvel participation remained resilient at 31%, funeral policies at 32%, and money transfer activity at 33% for both receiving and sending[10]. These collective savings and credit mechanisms allow consumers to make larger purchases or manage cash flow through social coordination rather than individual accumulation. The resilience of these traditional mechanisms demonstrates continued appetite for collective action even as individual purchasing patterns shift toward smaller, more frequent transactions.

### 3.5 Credit Usage Patterns

Credit usage patterns have evolved significantly during the 2023-2026 period, reflecting both improved access through lower interest rates and continued caution among consumers wary of accumulating additional debt. The interaction between these forces has produced a complex picture of credit market dynamics.

TransUnion research revealed that 91% of South Africans view credit as important for achieving financial goals, yet only 42% believe they have adequate access to credit[15]. This perception gap—between desire for credit and belief in accessibility—reflects the tight lending standards that have prevailed even as interest rates declined. Of those who considered applying for credit, 44% decided against proceeding, with high costs (33%), fear of rejection due to credit history (26%), and income concerns (24%) cited as primary barriers[15].

The composition of credit growth shows notable shifts. RCS data indicates that account numbers surged 14.3% year-on-year to 7.63 million, but average loan size dropped 11.4%[17]. This pattern suggests consumers are accessing credit more frequently but in smaller amounts, potentially using credit to manage cash flow rather than for major purchases. The top loan reason in 2024, cited by 38% of borrowers, was paying bills, food, and medical expenses—a dramatic shift from historical patterns where education, vehicles, and home improvements dominated[10].

Buy Now, Pay Later (BNPL) products have achieved remarkable penetration, with 57% of South Africans holding BNPL accounts and 36% having used BNPL multiple times in the past year[4]. BNPL has become particularly significant in fashion and electronics, where it drives 30-40% increases in average order value[11]. While BNPL can facilitate responsible consumption by allowing installment payments, the Credit Association of South Africa has raised concerns about illegal lenders gaining share as financial pressure intensifies and formal credit access remains constrained[4].

Younger generations demonstrate notably different credit orientations. Gen Z shows 42% intent to seek new credit, the highest of any generation, compared to Millennials at 39%, Gen X at 33%, and Baby Boomers at just 9%[15]. This generational divergence suggests that credit access will remain an important consumer finance topic even as older cohorts prioritize debt reduction and savings.

## 4. Digital Adoption Acceleration

### 4.1 Online Shopping Frequency Changes

Online shopping frequency has increased substantially between 2023 and 2026, driven by improved digital infrastructure, expanded retailer offerings, changing consumer preferences, and the residual behavioral shifts from the COVID-19 pandemic. South Africa's e-commerce market reached R225 billion in 2025, growing at 15% annually and outpacing traditional retail by a factor of three[11].

The penetration of online shopping is remarkable in context. Approximately 78% of South Africans now shop online at least once monthly, representing one of the highest rates of any developing market[11]. Internet penetration stands at 85% of the population, with smartphone ownership exceeding 95% among adults in urban areas—creating the foundation for widespread digital commerce adoption[11]. These infrastructure metrics compare favorably with many middle-income countries and provide the basis for continued digital commerce expansion.

The composition of online shopping has evolved significantly. Grocery delivery has emerged as a particularly dynamic segment, growing at 55% year-on-year as consumers discovered or rediscovered the convenience of online grocery shopping[11]. Fresh food delivery has similarly grown at 35%, reflecting improvements in cold chain logistics and consumer trust in online fresh produce quality. Health and beauty e-commerce has grown at 38%, while home improvement online sales have expanded at 42%[11].

Regional distribution of e-commerce shows concentration in economic hubs. Gauteng accounts for 42% of e-commerce volume, followed by Western Cape at 25% and KwaZulu-Natal at 18%[11]. However, the most dynamic growth is occurring outside these traditional centers, with township e-commerce growing at 22% annually—above the national average—and rural e-commerce expanding at over 50% annually[11]. This frontier growth suggests that digital commerce is extending into previously underserved areas, creating new market opportunities.

The festive season of 2025 provided a particularly vivid illustration of online shopping acceleration. Visa data showed online retail spending jumped 49.9% year-on-year during the 2025 festive season, confirming a step-change in digital commerce adoption[12]. This dramatic growth reflects both consumer preference shifts and retailer investments in digital capabilities, with platforms like Takealot Group, Woolworths Digital, and Checkers Sixty60 all reporting strong performance.

### 4.2 Mobile Commerce Growth

Mobile commerce has achieved dominant position in South African e-commerce, with mobile transactions accounting for 85% of all online purchases[11]. This overwhelming mobile preference has profound implications for retail strategy, requiring mobile-first design and optimized experiences as the default rather than supplementary channel.

The mobile dominance reflects South Africa's digital landscape, where smartphone ownership is nearly universal in urban areas and feature phones are increasingly smartphone-capable. The combination of high connectivity, smartphone penetration, and consumer preference for convenience has created an environment where mobile commerce is the natural rather than exceptional path for online shopping.

Quick commerce has emerged as a particularly dynamic mobile-enabled segment. Checkers Sixty60's reported 1.5 million weekly orders demonstrates the scale that rapid delivery platforms have achieved[11]. These platforms leverage mobile technology for order placement, real-time tracking, and gig-economy labor coordination, creating seamless consumer experiences that drive repeat usage. The success of quick commerce has prompted expansion by Takealot Group and other major players, intensifying competition in major metropolitan areas.

Mobile payment adoption has accelerated in parallel with mobile commerce. QR-based payments through SnapScan and Zapper account for 22% of digital transactions, while alternative payment methods overall represent 40% of online transactions[11]. The remaining 60% using traditional card payments still predominantly occurs via mobile devices, whether through saved card details in apps or mobile wallet integration. This payment infrastructure development has reduced friction in mobile commerce and contributed to transaction growth.

Looking forward, mobile commerce shows every indication of maintaining its dominant position. Research indicates that mobile-first shopping will continue to characterize South African retail, with social commerce, quick commerce, and offline stores converging into a single mobile-enabled ecosystem[14]. The strategic implication for retailers is clear: mobile optimization is no longer optional but essential for market relevance.

### 4.3 Social Commerce Emergence

Social commerce has emerged as a distinct and rapidly growing channel during the 2023-2026 period, with social shopping growing at 35% year-on-year[11]. WhatsApp and Facebook Shops have become significant sales platforms, particularly for smaller retailers and informal businesses, leveraging South Africa's high WhatsApp penetration and social media engagement.

The growth of social commerce reflects the evolution of consumer expectations around discovery and purchase pathways. Consumers increasingly expect instant, personalized shopping experiences that integrate social recommendations, user reviews, and seamless purchasing within social platforms[14]. This convergence of social engagement and commercial activity has created new opportunities for brands to reach consumers through influencer partnerships, community building, and direct social sales.

Gen Z consumers demonstrate particularly high social commerce engagement, with daily social media time averaging 3 hours and daily content messages scrolled reaching 10,000[13]. This intensive social media engagement creates natural opportunities for social commerce, particularly for categories like fashion, beauty, and entertainment that resonate with younger consumers. The challenge for brands is capturing attention within a cluttered social environment where the competition for engagement is intense.

Traditional retail has not been passive in responding to social commerce growth. Major South African retailers have invested in retail media networks that leverage first-party customer data for targeted advertising across social platforms[14]. This development blurs the distinction between social commerce and traditional digital retail, as retailers deploy sophisticated data capabilities to compete for consumer attention within social environments.

### 4.4 Digital Payment Adoption

Digital payment adoption has accelerated substantially during the period, driven by improved infrastructure, enhanced security, expanded options, and changing consumer preferences. The payment landscape has become notably more diverse, with traditional card payments now representing less than 60% of online transactions[11].

QR code payments have achieved significant scale, with SnapScan and Zapper collectively capturing 22% of digital transactions[11]. These contactless payment methods offer convenience and security that appeals to consumers wary of card fraud or seeking faster checkout experiences. The merchant network for QR payments has expanded substantially, covering major retailers, informal traders, and everything in between.

Buy Now, Pay Later has become a major payment category, particularly for fashion and electronics where BNPL accounts for 25% of purchases[11]. The growth of BNPL has been enabled by fintech innovation, with platforms like PayFast, PayJustNow, and others providing installment payment infrastructure that reduces risk for merchants while enabling consumers to manage cash flow. The 30-40% increase in average order value associated with BNPL demonstrates its commercial significance[11].

Cryptocurrency has achieved notable merchant acceptance, with over 30% of South African merchants now accepting cryptocurrency payments[11]. While cryptocurrency remains a niche payment method, this adoption rate positions South Africa among leading markets for crypto commerce. The appeal of cryptocurrency for some consumers includes protection against currency devaluation, transaction privacy, and borderless commerce capabilities.

Biometric authentication has emerged as an important security innovation, with adoption linked to 65% fraud reduction in digital payments[11]. The integration of fingerprint and facial recognition into mobile devices and payment apps has reduced friction while improving security, supporting broader digital payment adoption. This security improvement has been particularly important for building consumer trust in mobile commerce and digital financial services.

### 4.5 App-Based Retail Engagement

App-based retail engagement has deepened significantly during the 2023-2026 period, with consumers increasingly committed to specific retail apps that provide convenience, personalization, and loyalty rewards. This app engagement represents a qualitative shift in the consumer-retailer relationship.

Subscription models have achieved remarkable penetration, with 45% of South African consumers now using subscription services[11]. These range from streaming entertainment to meal kits to curated product boxes. Subscription commerce provides retailers with predictable revenue streams and valuable data on consumer preferences, while offering consumers convenience and often cost savings compared to ad-hoc purchasing.

Delivery tracking exemplifies the app-engagement pattern. Approximately 85% of consumers check delivery status three or more times per order[11], representing intensive app engagement that extends beyond the transaction itself. This tracking behavior reflects both the perceived risk of online purchases and the entertainment value of monitoring delivery progress. Retailers have responded by investing in real-time tracking capabilities that enhance the consumer experience.

Personalized shopping pathways driven by artificial intelligence have become standard among leading retailers. Research shows that personalized shopping experiences generate 40% higher conversion rates[11], creating strong incentives for retailers to invest in recommendation engines and targeted marketing. The competitive dynamics of app-based retail increasingly center on data capabilities and personalization effectiveness.

Voice commerce has emerged as a growing channel, with 18% of consumers using voice assistants for shopping and the category growing at 45% annually[11]. While still relatively nascent, voice commerce represents the next frontier of app-based retail, potentially enabling hands-free shopping for busy consumers or those with accessibility needs.

## 5. Values and Priorities

### 5.1 Price vs Quality Trade-offs

The price versus quality trade-off has been reshaped fundamentally during the 2023-2026 period, with price considerations ascending to unprecedented importance in consumer decision-making while quality remains a differentiating factor in specific contexts. This shift reflects both circumstantial pressure and lasting changes in consumer prioritization.

NIQ research identified affordability and low pricing as the single biggest reason for brand choice among South African shoppers[14]. This finding represents a significant departure from historical patterns where brand reputation, product quality, and other factors often superseded price in consumer decision-making. The elevation of price as the primary decision factor creates challenging dynamics for brands attempting to maintain premium positioning.

The expression of price-quality trade-offs varies significantly by category. In staple categories like groceries and household essentials, consumers demonstrate high price sensitivity and willingness to trade down for acceptable quality. In discretionary categories like cosmetics and personal care, quality considerations retain more influence, though price sensitivity has increased[10]. Durable goods occupy an intermediate position, where consumers seek value but remain willing to invest in products perceived as reliable and long-lasting.

The concept of "slow fashion" among younger consumers represents an interesting twist on the price-quality dynamic. Gen Z research reveals preferences for durable, high-quality "investment pieces" over fast fashion, even if this requires higher upfront costs[13]. This counter-trend suggests that for some consumers, particularly younger generations concerned with sustainability and long-term value, quality considerations may override price in specific purchase contexts.

Private label growth reflects the mainstream resolution of price-quality trade-offs. Consumers have discovered that private label products often deliver acceptable quality at substantially lower prices than branded alternatives. Retailers have responded by expanding private label offerings and improving quality to narrow the gap with national brands. This private label premiumization has benefited retailers while challenging branded manufacturers.

### 5.2 South African vs International Brand Preferences

Preference between South African and international brands has evolved during this period, shaped by patriotic sentiment, price differentials, quality improvements in local offerings, and evolving notions of authenticity. The picture is more nuanced than simple national preference, with context and category determining brand origin effects.

Research indicates that South African consumers increasingly favor brands that demonstrate local relevance, cultural authenticity, and community connection[18]. Brands sharing local stories and supporting communities resonate deeply, reflecting a broader trend toward value alignment in brand relationships. This authenticity premium has benefited local manufacturers and retailers who can credibly claim South African heritage.

The rise of international e-commerce has complicated the South African versus international brand dynamic. Access to international brands through Takealot Group's global assortment, Amazon South Africa's entry, and direct international shipping has increased competitive pressure on local brands[11]. Price gaps between local and international offerings have narrowed for some categories, increasing competitive intensity.

Support for local brands receives strong endorsement in survey research, though price and quality considerations often override patriotic preferences in actual purchase behavior. The distinction between stated preference for local brands and revealed preference through purchasing creates cognitive dissonance that brands must navigate carefully. Consumers may express support for local industry while making purchases based primarily on value considerations.

Certain categories show stronger local brand preference than others. Food and beverage categories with strong South African heritage, such as certain snacks and beverages, maintain robust local brand loyalty. Apparel brands with South African cultural resonance also perform well. By contrast, electronics and technology categories show stronger international brand preferences, reflecting global brand equity and limited local manufacturing capability.

### 5.3 Sustainability Considerations

Sustainability considerations have gained prominence in South African consumer psychology during the 2023-2026 period, though this elevation occurs within the constraints of price sensitivity and economic pressure. The relationship between sustainability and consumer behavior is complex, with stated intentions often diverging from revealed preferences.

Research indicates that 65% of South African consumers prefer eco-friendly options when shopping[11], suggesting meaningful underlying demand for sustainable products. However, this preference must be understood in context. PwC's Voice of the Consumer 2025 found that only 44% of South African consumers are willing to pay more for environmentally supportive food products[20]. This gap between stated preference and willingness-to-pay reveals the constraint that price sensitivity imposes on sustainability aspirations.

The economic pressure of the 2023-2026 period has made pure sustainability purchasing impractical for many households. Consumers focused on essential spending and value maximization have limited capacity to pay sustainability premiums. This economic constraint has particularly affected lower-income consumers, who face the paradox of being most exposed to environmental risks but least able to afford sustainable choices.

Certain sustainability practices have achieved broader adoption despite economic pressure. Reducing food waste, minimizing single-use plastics, and choosing products with recyclable packaging represent sustainability actions that often align with cost reduction rather than requiring premium payment. The growth of circular economy initiatives, upcycling, and second-hand shopping reflects both environmental awareness and economic necessity.

Younger consumers demonstrate notably stronger sustainability orientation. Gen Z research shows concern for environmental issues and preference for brands demonstrating sustainability commitment[13]. However, even among younger consumers, sustainability must compete with other values including price, quality, and authenticity. The expectation that Gen Z will drive sustainability through purchasing power may be overstated given their economic constraints.

### 5.4 Local and Community Support Trends

Local and community support has emerged as an important value driver during this period, reflecting both patriotic sentiment and pragmatic recognition of community economic interdependence. The concept of "supporting local" has gained cultural and political prominence, influencing consumer behavior across multiple categories.

Spaza shops and informal local retailers have benefited from community support trends, with township e-commerce growing at 22% annually—above the national average[11]. This growth reflects consumer preference for convenient local purchasing and, in some cases, perception of quality and service advantages from familiar local operators. The resilience of informal retail during economic stress demonstrates the community embeddedness of these businesses.

Stokvels and similar communal financial mechanisms demonstrate sophisticated local support systems that have remained robust during the period[10]. With 31% participation in stokvels and 32% holding funeral policies, communal financial behavior reflects deep tradition of mutual support within communities. These mechanisms provide both financial resilience and social connection, embodying local support values in concrete financial behavior.

Corporate community investment has received increased consumer attention, with brands expected to demonstrate local commitment beyond commercial activity. Research indicates that brands sharing local stories and supporting communities resonate more deeply with South African consumers[18]. This expectation creates both opportunity and risk for brands, with perceived inauthenticity or exploitation of community connection generating negative response.

The township economy has attracted increasing investment from formal retailers seeking to serve underserved markets. The expansion of retail networks into township areas, combined with the growth of township e-commerce, represents formal economy engagement with local community structures. This formal-informal integration may strengthen local economies while also potentially disrupting traditional informal business models.

### 5.5 Health and Wellness Focus

Health and wellness have maintained prominence in South African consumer psychology despite economic pressure, with certain health-related categories demonstrating sustained or growing consumer interest. This focus reflects both heightened health awareness following the COVID-19 pandemic and increasing chronic disease burden in South Africa.

Nutrition and healthy eating have received substantial consumer attention. Research indicates that 70% of consumers are prioritizing healthier food and drink options[14]. This health orientation is reflected in category growth patterns, with health and beauty e-commerce growing at 38%[11]. Demand for vitamins, supplements, and functional foods has expanded, with natural and organic skincare achieving peak search volumes in January 2026[21].

Anti-obesity medications have begun bringing widespread disruption to food and beverage categories[14]. This pharmaceutical intervention in consumer eating behavior represents a profound shift with significant implications for food manufacturers, retailers, and the broader food system. Categories associated with weight management, portion control, and nutritional supplementation may benefit from this trend.

Mental health awareness has achieved greater cultural prominence, with younger generations particularly attuned to mental wellness concerns. Research shows that 40% of Gen Z consumers experience anxiety or burnout[13], driving interest in wellness-oriented products and services. The "slow living" movement among young South Africans reflects prioritization of mental health and meaningful experiences over material consumption.

Alcohol consumption trends downward, particularly among younger generations[14]. This decline reflects multiple factors including health consciousness, religious observance, and changing social norms around drinking. The implications for alcohol retailers and producers are significant, with premium alcohol particularly exposed to demand erosion.

## 6. Life Stage and Demographic Changes

### 6.1 Young Professionals (Millennials/Gen Z) Behavior

Young professionals, encompassing both Millennials and Generation Z, demonstrate distinct behavioral patterns that are reshaping South African consumer psychology. These generations exhibit higher optimism than older cohorts while simultaneously facing severe economic pressures, creating a complex psychological profile.

Gen Z consumers, defined as those aged 13-28 comprising 19 million people or 29% of South Africa's population, represent a substantial and growing market force[13]. Their projected spending power of R1.1 trillion annually by 2030 positions this generation as increasingly influential in consumer markets. Their household purchasing influence exceeds 80% of family decisions, extending their economic impact beyond direct personal consumption[13].

Financial discipline characterizes young South African consumer behavior despite modest incomes. Many Gen Z consumers earn under R5,000 monthly yet manage to save an average of R1,800 monthly[13]. This disciplined approach reflects both economic necessity and financial awareness, though the average savings may conceal significant variation and potential assistance from family networks.

Side hustles and informal work have become normative among young South Africans, with over 60% juggling informal work alongside primary employment to cope with rising costs[13]. This multi-income approach represents adaptive response to economic pressure, with implications for consumption patterns, time allocation, and career development. The informal economy provides flexibility and supplementary income but may also indicate inadequate formal employment opportunities.

Digital native behavior defines young South Africans' consumer engagement. Daily phone time averaging 7 hours, with 3 hours on social media, demonstrates intensive digital engagement[13]. This digital immersion creates natural receptivity to social commerce, mobile commerce, and digital financial services. Brands seeking to reach younger consumers must meet them in digital spaces with relevant, authentic content.

Gen Z demonstrates sophisticated brand relationships characterized by conditional loyalty. Research shows 61% would consider themselves lifelong customers if brands deliver on authenticity and quality[13]. However, 73% are willing to pay more for value-aligned products, not just trends, indicating that younger consumers actively curate their brand relationships based on brand behavior rather than simply choosing cheapest options.

### 6.2 Middle Class Contraction and Expansion

The trajectory of South Africa's middle class during 2023-2026 presents a complex picture of simultaneous contraction and expansion pressures, with net effects varying by geographic location and economic sector. Understanding middle class dynamics is essential for businesses targeting this historically important consumer segment.

The broader middle class has faced significant erosion pressures. Real disposable income has declined despite nominal increases, with household buffers being depleted through the sustained economic stress period[10]. The 48% drop in saving and investing capacity between 2021 and 2024 indicates that middle class households have been drawing down accumulated assets to maintain consumption standards[10]. This asset depletion raises questions about medium-term consumption capacity.

Simultaneously, certain segments of the middle class have demonstrated resilience or expansion. Higher-income households have benefited from asset price appreciation, particularly in residential property where rand values surged 17% in 2025[19]. These wealth effects have supported consumer confidence and discretionary spending among upper-income brackets, creating a bifurcated middle class experience.

The structural composition of the middle class has shifted toward value-oriented consumption. Discount retail growth, with Boxer's 12% sales increase and 16% trading profit growth, suggests middle class migration toward value formats[21]. The expansion of Takealot Group's mid-market offerings and the growth of private label indicate middle class appetite for quality at accessible price points.

Employment dynamics have disproportionately affected middle class formation. The unemployment rate's fluctuation between 31.4% and 33.2% during this period indicates limited job creation in formal sector employment[8]. Youth unemployment at 43.8% represents particularly severe constraints on middle class entry for younger households[8]. The absence of employment growth limits middle class expansion through new household formation.

Geographic differentiation characterizes middle class evolution. Major metropolitan areas, particularly those with stronger economic growth, have maintained more robust middle class presence. Smaller towns and rural areas have experienced greater middle class erosion as economic opportunities remain limited. This geographic divergence has implications for retail network strategies and market targeting.

### 6.3 Retiree Spending Patterns

Retiree spending patterns have evolved during the 2023-2026 period, reflecting the intersection of aging population demographics, pension system dynamics, and changing consumption priorities. While specific retiree-focused research is limited in reviewed sources, available evidence suggests distinctive patterns among older South Africans.

Retirement saving priorities have intensified among older consumers. Research shows 38% of consumers plan to increase retirement savings and investment contributions, with this priority particularly strong among older generations[15]. This increased saving focus reduces current consumption capacity but may reflect rational response to uncertainty about future pension adequacy, particularly given the two-pot retirement system changes that have altered preservation and access options.

Credit behavior among retirees shows notably lower intent compared to younger generations. Only 9% of Baby Boomers plan to seek new credit or refinance, compared to 42% of Gen Z[15]. This conservative credit orientation suggests that retirees prioritize debt reduction and financial stability over consumption financed through borrowing. The relatively low credit penetration among retirees may reflect both adequate pension income and risk aversion.

Healthcare spending represents an expanding category for retirees. The aging of South Africa's population increases demand for healthcare products and services, while inflation in healthcare costs has outpaced general inflation[4]. This healthcare cost pressure reduces discretionary spending capacity while potentially improving health outcomes and quality of life.

Intergenerational financial flows increasingly characterize retiree economic engagement. Grandparents supporting adult children and grandchildren through direct transfers, childcare provision, and household cost sharing represent significant economic activity that may not appear in standard consumption statistics. These intergenerational transfers both reflect and reinforce family cohesion while potentially constraining retiree consumption.

### 6.4 Family Structure Impacts

Family structure impacts on consumer psychology have received limited direct attention in the reviewed sources, though available evidence suggests meaningful variations by household composition. The increasing diversity of family structures in South Africa creates complex consumer segments with distinct needs and behaviors.

Female-headed households face particularly severe economic pressure. Research shows food insecurity deepening in South Africa, with crisis most severe in Eastern Cape and particularly affecting female-headed households[8]. This gendered dimension of economic stress has profound implications for consumption patterns, priorities, and coping strategies within these households.

Multi-generational household formation has increased as economic pressure forces adult children to remain with or return to parents. This household extension creates shared resource pooling but also potential conflict over consumption priorities. The dynamics of multi-generational households differ substantially from nuclear family or single-person households, requiring different retail and service approaches.

Single-parent households, predominantly female-headed, represent a growing segment facing concentrated economic pressure. The combination of single income, childcare costs, and limited time creates distinctive consumption patterns emphasizing convenience, value, and essential categories. Quick commerce and delivery services may find particular resonance with time-constrained single parents.

Extended family networks beyond coresidential households play significant economic roles. The prevalence of money transfers, with 33% of households both receiving and sending money transfers[10], indicates extensive non-coresidential family economic engagement. These networks create both obligations and safety nets, influencing disposable income and consumption priorities.

## 7. External Factor Impacts

### 7.1 Loadshedding Influence on Shopping Behavior

Loadshedding, the scheduled electricity outages implemented due to Eskom generation constraints, has profoundly influenced South African consumer behavior throughout the 2023-2026 period, though relief has emerged in recent months. The impact extends across multiple consumption categories and behavioral patterns.

The relationship between loadshedding and retail behavior operates through multiple channels. Research from NielsenIQ documented that the pause in Eskom load shedding in Q2 2024 enabled significant improvements in fresh food category growth of 8.2% and frozen food category growth of 9%[14]. These gains reflect consumers' ability to properly store temperature-sensitive foods when electricity supply is reliable. The absence of loadshedding similarly supported home appliance turnaround, with 11.3% growth compared to 6.1% decline in Q2 2023[14].

Grocery shopping behavior has adapted to loadshedding uncertainty. Consumers have developed coping strategies including shopping during powered periods, prioritizing chilled and frozen purchases when supply is available, and shifting toward shelf-stable products during extended outages. The normalization of these adaptations may persist even as loadshedding declines, with implications for cold chain demand and shelf-stable category preferences.

Media consumption has been substantially affected by loadshedding. Traditional media viewing declines during power outages as consumers lose access to television, creating ripple effects for advertising revenue and media consumption patterns[14]. Streaming services face similar challenges when power interruptions disrupt viewing. This media consumption disruption has accelerated digital media adoption and created demand for uninterruptible power solutions.

The decline in loadshedding since 2024 represents meaningful relief for consumers and retailers. Research indicates that consumers gained confidence about the loadshedding situation, inspiring appliance upgrades including large screen TVs that outperformed outside traditional Black Friday/Festive season windows[14]. This replacement cycle benefits retailers while also improving consumer quality of life through enhanced home entertainment capability.

Energy sector reforms and the expansion of renewable energy offer prospects for sustained loadshedding reduction. Research indicates that outages have caused significant economic damage, though they notably declined in 2024[14]. The continued expansion of rooftop solar and battery storage among residential consumers provides further resilience against electricity supply disruption, though adoption remains concentrated among higher-income households.

### 7.2 Transport Costs and Fuel Price Effects

Transport costs and fuel prices have remained significant consumer pressure points during the 2023-2026 period, with volatility creating planning difficulty and persistent inflation eroding purchasing power for transport-dependent households. The impact extends across multiple consumption categories and daily decision-making.

Fuel price volatility has characterized the period with both increases and temporary relief. Ahead of the 2025 festive season, diesel prices increased by almost 90 cents per litre, prompting warnings from the Road Freight Association about impacts on everyday vehicle operating costs[7]. The fuel levy reduction announced in March 2026 provided one-month relief as government responded to price pressures from the Iran conflict[7]. This volatility creates challenges for consumer planning and business cost management.

Transport costs directly affect food prices through logistics cost pass-through. The 32% fuel cost surge affecting road freight and logistics creates direct cost increases for transported goods[7]. These logistics cost increases typically flow through to consumer prices with some lag, particularly for fresh produce and goods requiring temperature-controlled transport. The food security implications of transport cost increases are significant given already-elevated food insecurity.

Consumer shopping patterns have adapted to transport cost increases. The growth of township e-commerce at 22% annually and rural e-commerce at over 50% reflects partly reduced willingness or ability to travel to distant retail locations[11]. Quick commerce and delivery services benefit from this trend, though delivery costs may offset fuel savings for some consumers. The net effect on retail geography remains evolving.

Vehicle operating cost concerns have accelerated interest in electric vehicles despite limited current adoption. Research shows rising fuel costs pushing South Africans to consider electric vehicles as alternatives to internal combustion engine cars[7]. This attitudinal shift may precede actual adoption as EV infrastructure and affordability improve, representing potential long-term change in vehicle purchase behavior.

### 7.3 Food Security Concerns

Food security concerns have deepened during the 2023-2026 period, with multiple indicators pointing to increased vulnerability particularly among lower-income households and specific geographic regions. The intersection of food price inflation, unemployment, and climate-related production challenges has created persistent food security pressures.

Statistics South Africa's Food Security Report for 2019, 2022, and 2023 documented substantial food insecurity prevalence even before the full impact of 2023-2026 economic conditions[8]. The projection that approximately half of South Africans may not have enough to eat by 2025 indicates the severity of underlying food security challenges[8]. This baseline vulnerability has been exacerbated by economic pressures during the review period.

Research from Forbes Africa reveals deepening food insecurity in South Africa in 2025, with crisis most severe in Eastern Cape and particularly affecting female-headed households[8]. Geographic concentration of food insecurity creates particular challenges for relief efforts and retail network development. The Eastern Cape's combination of limited economic opportunity and agricultural constraints creates persistent vulnerability.

Dietary diversity has emerged as a particular concern within food security discussions. The Shoprite Group's SA Food Security Index 2025 indicates increasing attention to dietary diversity beyond mere caloric adequacy[8]. This nutritional focus reflects recognition that food security encompasses not just quantity but quality of diet, with implications for fresh produce demand and nutrition-oriented retail formats.

Food price inflation dynamics have provided some relief, with Statistics South Africa reporting annual consumer price inflation of just 2.8% in April 2025[7]. This moderation from peak levels supports food purchasing power, though elevated food prices relative to 2020 baselines maintain pressure on household budgets. Poultry price increases due to import bans created additional challenges in 2025[7].

Climate impacts on South African agriculture have introduced production uncertainty. Late rains affecting summer crop harvests in 2025 illustrate the weather-related production risks facing local agriculture[7]. These climate pressures may persist, creating ongoing supply uncertainty for key food categories and potentially supporting elevated food price volatility.

### 7.4 Job Security Anxieties

Job security anxieties have remained elevated throughout the 2023-2026 period, with the South African labor market characterized by persistently high unemployment that creates pervasive uncertainty even for those currently employed. These anxieties influence consumer psychology and spending behavior in profound ways.

The official unemployment rate fluctuated between 31.4% and 33.2% during the period, representing among the highest rates globally[8]. The expanded unemployment rate, which includes discouraged work-seekers, reached 42.4% in Q3 2025[8]. This massive labor market slack creates pervasive job insecurity even for those in employment, as workers recognize that job loss would mean extremely prolonged search given available opportunities.

Youth unemployment at 43.8% represents particularly acute concern[8]. Young South Africans entering the labor market face extremely limited formal employment prospects, driving the side hustle culture and informal work participation observed among Gen Z[13]. This youth unemployment crisis has long-term implications for consumption capacity and social stability.

The concentration of job losses across sectors has created specific anxieties. Statistics South Africa documented job losses in six of ten industries during the economic wrap-up for August 2025[8]. This sectoral concentration means that workers in affected industries face particularized risks beyond general labor market weakness. Workers in these sectors demonstrate heightened job search activity and reduced consumption.

Concerns about credit access reflect underlying job security anxieties. Research shows that 24% of consumers cite income or employment concerns as barriers to credit access[15]. Fear of job loss amplifies reluctance to take on additional debt obligations, contributing to the credit restraint observed even as interest rates decline. This cautious approach may persist beyond actual employment conditions.

The interplay between job security and consumption is complex. Some consumers maintain or increase spending despite job insecurity, perhaps driven by necessity or optimism, while others dramatically reduce spending in anticipation of potential unemployment. This behavioral divergence creates challenges for forecasting consumption patterns and retail demand.

### 7.5 Property and Asset Values

Property and asset values have shown notable recovery during the 2023-2026 period, with residential property particularly demonstrating strong appreciation that has supported household wealth and consumer confidence among homeowners. This asset price recovery creates divergent experiences between property owners and those without property assets.

The rand value of properties surged 17% in 2025, described as a "really positive end to the year, which sets a strong base"[19]. This appreciation reflects multiple factors including limited supply, demand from higher-income buyers, and improved market sentiment following interest rate reductions. The aggregate value of South Africa's residential property market reached R543.42 billion in 2026[19].

National nominal house price growth ran at approximately 4% to 6% during the period, with modest real recovery after years of stagnation[19]. December 2025 showed 3.2% growth compared to the same period in the prior year, with meaningfully improved affordability as interest rates declined[19]. The trajectory suggests potential for continued recovery, though structural constraints limit the pace.

Sectional title properties have gained momentum as house prices climb, reflecting changing preferences and urban densification[19]. Foreign investment in South African residential property has provided additional demand support, with average foreign purchase price of R2.7 million per home[19]. This foreign interest signals confidence in South African property markets despite domestic economic challenges.

The wealth effect from property appreciation supports consumer confidence and spending among homeowners. Research notes that South Africans grew their wealth at a much faster rate than incomes in 2025, with household assets less debt 15% larger[19]. This wealth accumulation provides a buffer against economic uncertainty and may support consumption even as income growth remains subdued.

Lower-income households face limited participation in property-driven wealth effects. Those without property assets or those whose property values have not appreciated similarly cannot benefit from these wealth effects, creating divergent economic experiences across the wealth distribution. This divergence has contributed to the income-group bifurcation in consumer confidence noted earlier.

## 8. Future Outlook

### 8.1 Consumer Optimism and Pessimism Indicators

The outlook for South African consumer psychology reflects a complex interplay of improved macroeconomic conditions and deeply rooted structural challenges. Current indicators suggest cautious optimism with significant downside risks, creating a nuanced picture for businesses and policymakers.

Near-term consumer confidence appears to be stabilizing at improved levels. The FNB/BER Consumer Confidence Index recovery to -7 in Q1 2026, the highest reading since Q4 2024, indicates reduced pessimism[1][2]. This improvement reflects lower interest rates, improved retail performance, and better-than-expected economic management. The second consecutive quarterly increase suggests momentum toward more positive territory.

Forward-looking expectations remain cautious. Despite current improvements, forecasts project the index at -11 at Q1 2026 end, -10 in 2027, and -11 in 2028[2]. This suggests that sustained recovery to positive territory remains uncertain and dependent on continued economic improvement. Geopolitical risks, particularly potential Middle East/Iranian conflict ripple effects, could cause "U-turn in high- and middle-income confidence during Q2 2026"[2].

Household financial expectations show striking divergence from present conditions. Research shows 72% of South Africans feel positive about their financial outlook for the next 12 months, while 75% expect income to increase[4]. This expressed optimism coexists with active financial restructuring and defensive behaviors, suggesting that optimism may reflect adaptive coping rather than current economic reality. The psychological gap between expressed optimism and actual behavior is notable.

Youth and higher-income optimism lead the improvement. Gen Z and Millennial optimism levels exceed older generations, as does high-income household confidence[15]. This generational and income divergence suggests that overall optimism statistics may overstate typical consumer experience, particularly given the concentration of economic pressure among lower-income groups.

### 8.2 Recovery Expectations

Expectations for economic recovery vary substantially across sources and scenarios, with baseline forecasts suggesting modest improvement but significant downside risks. The trajectory of recovery will shape consumer psychology profoundly and determine the durability of behavioral changes observed during the stress period.

GDP growth forecasts suggest gradual improvement. Economists project South African growth at approximately 1.5-1.6% for 2026, up from 1.2% in 2025[6]. The OECD forecasts more optimistic scenarios at 1.3-1.4% for 2025-2026[9]. While these growth rates remain below levels needed for substantial employment creation, they represent improvement from recent stagnation.

Household consumption recovery appears underway. Research notes that household consumption grew by 3.1% in 2025 compared to 1.0% in 2024[6]. This improvement reflects both pent-up demand following the tax shock and the supportive effects of lower interest rates and moderating inflation. The composition of this growth, particularly the balance between essential and discretionary categories, will reveal much about consumer psychology durability.

Inflation expectations remain anchored near target. The SARB's inflation forecasts show CPI at 3.2% in 2026, below the 4.5% target[9]. This disinflation provides monetary policy flexibility to support growth while maintaining price stability. Should inflation remain subdued, further interest rate reductions could provide additional consumer relief.

Structural constraints limit recovery potential. Infrastructure challenges including Eskom capacity, logistics constraints, and skills shortages create supply-side limits on growth that monetary policy cannot address. These structural factors may perpetuate elevated unemployment and constrain middle class expansion, maintaining defensive consumer psychology even during macroeconomic improvement.

### 8.3 Spending Intention Surveys

Spending intention surveys reveal divergent patterns across demographic groups and consumption categories, with some consumers planning increased spending while others maintain defensive postures. Understanding these intentions provides insight into likely consumption trajectory.

Category-specific intentions show essential orientation. Research indicates continued preference for essential over discretionary categories, with health-conscious and value-oriented shopping priorities[21]. Promotions and loyalty programs remain effective in encouraging repeat purchases, suggesting that price sensitivity will persist as a primary behavior driver.

Younger consumers show increased spending intentions on digital services and discretionary activities[15]. This differential represents both greater optimism among youth and their particular consumption preferences. Gen Z's high optimism and digital orientation suggests that entertainment, technology, and experience categories may outperform among younger consumers even if overall discretionary spending remains constrained.

Retirement and savings intentions show increased prudence across generations. The 38% of consumers planning increased retirement savings reflects widespread recognition of financial fragility[15]. This increased saving reduces current consumption but may provide future security that supports sustainable spending. The balance between saving and consumption represents a critical long-term consumer psychology question.

Debt management intentions remain prominent. The 35% of consumers accelerating debt repayments suggests continued deleveraging that will reduce interest burdens over time but constrain current consumption[15]. The interaction between debt reduction and consumption represents a key transmission mechanism from household balance sheet repair to spending capacity recovery.

### 8.4 Category-Level Projections

Category-level projections for South African retail suggest continued divergence between essential and discretionary categories, with digital and value-oriented formats outperforming. These projections inform strategic planning across the retail sector.

E-commerce category growth is projected to continue. The market is expected to reach US$77.27 billion by 2034 from US$38.11 billion in 2025, representing 8.17% compound growth[11]. This sustained growth reflects continued digital adoption, improved logistics, and persistent consumer preference for convenience. Physical retail will maintain majority share but face persistent competitive pressure from digital channels.

Fresh food and grocery delivery show strong momentum. The 55% growth in grocery delivery and 35% growth in fresh food delivery demonstrate consumer comfort with online grocery formats[11]. Continued investment in cold chain logistics and micro-fulfillment capabilities will determine whether this growth potential is realized.

Health and beauty categories demonstrate robust growth potential. The 38% e-commerce growth in health and beauty reflects consumer prioritization of personal care despite economic pressure[11]. Premium and masstige segments may perform differently, with value-oriented products gaining share while ultra-premium maintains among higher-income consumers.

Quick commerce has achieved scale but faces margin pressure. The 1.5 million weekly orders for Checkers Sixty60 demonstrates quick commerce market acceptance[11]. However, the economics of rapid delivery remain challenging, and sustainability of current business models requires continued efficiency improvement. Geographic expansion may be limited by infrastructure requirements.

Home improvement categories show recovery potential. The 42% growth in home improvement e-commerce reflects housing market activity and consumer investment in domestic environment[11]. Should property market recovery sustain, home improvement may benefit from wealth effects and consumer investment in property maintenance and enhancement.

## 9. Conclusion

The evolution of South African consumer psychology between 2023 and 2026 represents a period of profound transformation shaped by the intersection of macroeconomic adjustment, infrastructural challenges, and fundamental shifts in consumer values and behaviors. The evidence reviewed in this analysis reveals a consumer landscape that has been permanently altered by the stresses of this period, with behavioral changes that extend well beyond cyclical responses to temporary shocks.

Consumer confidence demonstrated remarkable volatility during this period, with the trajectory from negative territory through the -20 "tax shock" low in Q1 2025 to the -7 recovery in Q1 2026 illustrating both the vulnerability of consumer sentiment to policy changes and the resilience that characterizes South African households[1][2]. The bifurcation of confidence between high-income and lower-income groups reveals a recovery characterized by inequality, with asset price appreciation benefiting wealthier households while employment and income pressures persist for the majority.

The moderation of inflation from 6.08% to 3.0% has provided meaningful relief, but the distribution of benefits has been uneven[8][9]. Administered price increases in electricity, water, and other essentials have continued to outpace general inflation, constraining the real purchasing power gains from disinflation[4]. Price sensitivity has escalated to unprecedented levels, with affordability ranking as the primary brand choice factor[14]. This heightened price sensitivity may represent a permanent shift rather than a temporary adaptation.

Digital adoption has achieved critical mass during this period, with e-commerce reaching R225 billion and capturing 16% of total retail[11]. Mobile commerce dominance at 85% of transactions has established mobile-first as the default channel strategy[11]. Social commerce, quick commerce, and digital payments have emerged as distinct and growing channels. These digital shifts represent structural changes that will persist and evolve beyond the current period.

The behavioral changes observed extend beyond consumption to encompass fundamental approaches to financial management. Defensive consumption patterns, accelerated debt repayment, increased savings, and cautious credit behavior reflect transformed financial psychology[4][15]. While expressed optimism remains high, actual behaviors indicate risk aversion and contingency planning. The "making a plan" orientation that characterizes South African consumer psychology has found expression in systematic financial restructuring.

Looking forward, the outlook for South African consumer psychology involves both encouraging signs and persistent risks. The recovery trajectory in consumer confidence, continued disinflation, and interest rate reductions provide supportive conditions for improved consumption[2][9]. However, structural constraints including elevated unemployment, infrastructure challenges, and global uncertainty create downside risks. The durability of behavioral changes—whether consumers return to pre-stress patterns or maintain fundamentally altered approaches—remains uncertain.

For businesses operating in South African consumer markets, the implications are clear. Value-oriented strategies that deliver quality at accessible price points will likely succeed even as economic conditions improve. Mobile-first digital engagement has become essential rather than supplementary. Price sensitivity and promotional responsiveness require sophisticated pricing and loyalty strategies. The bifurcation between consumer segments demands differentiated approaches rather than one-size-fits-all marketing. Success in this environment requires deep understanding of South African consumer psychology in its current transformed state, not nostalgic assumptions about pre-2023 patterns.
