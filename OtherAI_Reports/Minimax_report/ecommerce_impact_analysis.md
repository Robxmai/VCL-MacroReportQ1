# The Impact of E-Commerce on Traditional Retail Sales in South Africa: A Comprehensive Analysis Report

## Executive Summary

South Africa's e-commerce sector has undergone a remarkable transformation over the past several years, evolving from a marginal channel accounting for less than 1% of total retail sales in 2019 to nearly 10% by 2025. This dramatic shift represents one of the most significant structural changes in the country's retail landscape and signals a fundamental reordering of consumer behavior and market dynamics. The online retail market grew by 35% in 2024 to reach R96 billion, with projections indicating it will exceed R130 billion in 2025, capturing approximately 10% of total retail turnover[1][2]. The growth trajectory has been extraordinary, with e-commerce expanding more than ten times faster than physical retail, which grew by only 2.5% in 2024 and 1.6% by mid-2025[1][3].

This analysis examines the multifaceted impact of e-commerce on traditional retail in South Africa, exploring growth statistics, consumer behavioral shifts, competitive dynamics, market structure changes, and employment effects. The findings reveal a retail sector at an inflection point, where digital platforms have become embedded in everyday consumer behavior while traditional retailers are compelled to adapt their strategies or risk marginalization. The competitive landscape has intensified with the entry of global players like Amazon in May 2024, while domestic platforms like Takealot maintain dominance and traditional supermarket chains have launched aggressive e-commerce delivery services. Meanwhile, Chinese platforms Shein and Temu have captured significant market share in the fashion segment, raising concerns about impacts on local manufacturing and retail employment, with up to 34,000 jobs potentially at risk by 2030[4][5].

## 1. Introduction

The retail sector in South Africa stands at a pivotal moment in its history. The convergence of improved internet connectivity, mobile technology adoption, changing consumer expectations, and the entry of global competitors has catalyzed an e-commerce revolution that is reshaping every aspect of how goods and services are bought and sold in the country. Understanding the scope, nature, and implications of this transformation has become essential for policymakers, business leaders, investors, and consumers alike.

This report provides a comprehensive analysis of how e-commerce has impacted traditional retail sales in South Africa. The analysis draws upon multiple authoritative sources including government statistics from Stats SA, industry reports from World Wide Worx, research from Naspers and Mastercard, and independent market studies. The objective is to present a balanced assessment of the current state of affairs, identify key trends and patterns, and illuminate the challenges and opportunities facing both established traditional retailers and emerging digital players.

The scope of this analysis encompasses several interconnected domains. First, it examines the quantitative growth of e-commerce, including market size, growth rates, and penetration levels relative to total retail. Second, it explores the behavioral shifts driving consumer adoption of online shopping, including the motivations, preferences, and concerns that influence purchasing decisions. Third, it analyzes the competitive responses from traditional retailers, the emergence of new market entrants, and the evolution of delivery and payment infrastructure. Fourth, it considers the broader structural implications for employment, market consolidation, and the retail property sector. Finally, it discusses the regulatory environment and the challenges posed by international e-commerce platforms.

## 2. E-Commerce Growth Statistics and Market Dynamics

### 2.1 Market Size and Growth Trajectory

The growth of e-commerce in South Africa over the past half-decade represents one of the most significant retail transformations in the nation's economic history. When measured against the baseline of 2019, when online retail accounted for less than 1% of total retail sales, the current landscape appears almost unrecognizable. By 2025, e-commerce has reached approximately 10% of total retail turnover, a tenfold increase in market share within just six years[6][7]. This rapid expansion has moved South Africa from what the United Nations Conference on Trade and Development (UNCTAD) classifies as an "emerging" e-commerce market to an "expanding" one, placing it alongside Brazil, India, and China in terms of digital retail maturity[6].

The quantitative trajectory of growth reveals the accelerating pace of adoption. In 2023, total online sales reached R71 billion, representing 6% of national retail and achieving 29% year-on-year growth[8]. This already represented substantial expansion, but 2024 witnessed an even more dramatic acceleration, with online retail growing by 35% to reach R96 billion and capture 8% of total retail sales[1][2]. The momentum has continued into 2025, with annualized growth reaching 38% during the year, far outpacing the sluggish 2.5% growth recorded by physical retail in 2024 and the mere 1.6% growth observed in mid-2025[1][3]. By the end of 2025, online retail is projected to exceed R130 billion, representing approximately 10% of total retail sales[2][7]. Looking further ahead, industry projections suggest the market could surpass R150 billion by 2027, with e-commerce potentially accounting for 12% of total retail turnover by that year[1][6].

The broader market sizing figures paint an even more ambitious picture. According to market research firm Mordor Intelligence, the South Africa e-commerce market is expected to reach USD 38.13 billion in 2025, growing to USD 63.06 billion by 2031, representing a compound annual growth rate of approximately 8.54%[9]. These figures encompass the full spectrum of digital commerce, including business-to-consumer, consumer-to-consumer, and business-to-business transactions, and reflect the increasingly integral role that digital platforms play across the entire retail value chain.

### 2.2 Historical Context and Pandemic Effects

The contemporary e-commerce boom cannot be fully understood without appreciating the role of the COVID-19 pandemic as an accelerant. Prior to the pandemic, South Africa's e-commerce sector was growing steadily but remained modest in scale. Pre-pandemic e-commerce stood at approximately R20 billion in 2019[8]. The lockdown restrictions and social distancing measures implemented from 2020 onwards catalyzed a dramatic shift in consumer behavior, with many South Africans attempting online shopping for the first time. By 2021, COVID-era e-commerce had surged to over R40 billion, more than doubling the pre-pandemic level within just two years[8].

However, the post-pandemic period has revealed that this growth was not merely a temporary spike but rather a structural shift in consumer behavior. While growth rates moderated somewhat in 2023 following the explosive COVID-era expansion, the underlying trajectory remained firmly upward. The 29% growth in 2023 to R71 billion, the 35% growth in 2024 to R96 billion, and the projected 38% growth in 2025 to over R130 billion demonstrate a market that has established durable foundations for continued expansion[1][2][8]. The distinction between pandemic-driven necessity and long-term behavioral change is crucial: South African consumers have embraced e-commerce not merely as a substitute for in-store shopping during lockdowns but as a permanent complement to their retail activities.

### 2.3 International Comparisons and Growth Potential

While South Africa's e-commerce penetration has grown dramatically, the country still has substantial room for expansion relative to global benchmarks. In the United States, online retail accounts for approximately 15% of total retail sales, while in China the figure reaches approximately 30%[10]. South Africa's current level of nearly 10% positions it between these extremes, but significantly below both markets. This gap represents both a challenge and an opportunity. On one hand, it indicates that South Africa's e-commerce sector has not yet reached the saturation levels observed in more mature markets, suggesting continued growth potential. On the other hand, the gap reflects structural constraints including infrastructure limitations, payment access barriers, and logistical challenges that may temper the pace of future expansion.

The growth rate comparison is equally instructive. South Africa's consistent achievement of above-30% annual growth rates in e-commerce outpaces many developed markets and reflects the "catch-up" dynamics at play as the country leverages technological advancements and changing consumer attitudes. However, as the base level of e-commerce penetration increases, maintaining such growth rates will become progressively more challenging, requiring sustained investment in infrastructure, logistics, and digital literacy.

## 3. Consumer Shift Analysis

### 3.1 Motivations for Moving Online

The shift of South African consumers toward online shopping reflects a complex interplay of motivations, many of which are interrelated and mutually reinforcing. Understanding these motivations is essential for comprehending the structural drivers of e-commerce growth and the likely future evolution of the market.

Convenience has emerged as the dominant driver of online shopping adoption. The ability to browse products, compare prices, and complete purchases without leaving home or the office resonates strongly with consumers facing time constraints, long commutes, and increasingly busy schedules. This is particularly relevant in South Africa's urban centers, where traffic congestion can transform what should be a simple shopping trip into a significant time commitment. The on-demand nature of many newer e-commerce services, particularly grocery delivery platforms like Checkers Sixty60 and Woolworths' Woolies Dash, has further amplified the convenience proposition by offering delivery within hours of ordering[11].

Price comparison capability represents another powerful driver of online adoption. Digital platforms enable consumers to instantaneously compare prices across multiple sellers, search for deals and promotions, and access a wider range of price points than would be feasible in a physical store. While South African consumers have historically been highly price-sensitive, perhaps reflecting the country's high levels of inequality and the ongoing cost-of-living pressures, the importance of price as a primary motivation has actually been declining. Research indicates that the proportion of consumers who report shopping online primarily for lower prices fell from 18.9% in 2023 to 12.1% in 2024[12]. This counterintuitive finding suggests that as consumers become more comfortable with e-commerce, their motivations evolve beyond mere price-seeking toward appreciating the broader experience and assurance that online shopping can offer.

Product variety and availability constitute a third major motivation. Online platforms offer access to far broader product assortments than any physical store could stock, including international products and specialty items that would be difficult or impossible to find through traditional retail channels. This is particularly relevant for consumers in rural or peri-urban areas that may lack access to full-service retail outlets. The ability to source products from multiple vendors through a single marketplace platform simplifies the shopping process and reduces the need to visit multiple physical locations.

### 3.2 Research Before Purchase: The Showrooming Phenomenon

An important behavioral pattern that has emerged in South Africa's evolving retail landscape is the practice of researching products online before making purchases, whether online or in physical stores. This phenomenon, commonly referred to as "showrooming" when consumers examine products in stores before buying online, reflects the increasingly informed and empowered consumer.

Research indicates that 28.2% of South African consumers report using the internet to research products before making significant purchases, while 23.1% check multiple sources before making substantial purchasing decisions[12]. These figures demonstrate the profound impact of digital access on consumer information-seeking behavior. The implications for traditional retailers are complex: on one hand, consumers who research in-store may still complete their purchases at physical locations; on the other hand, the availability of comprehensive online information enables consumers to make more informed decisions and may facilitate online purchasing that might not otherwise have occurred.

The broader pattern suggests a convergence of online and offline consumer journeys rather than a wholesale migration from one channel to the other. Omnichannel strategies that integrate digital research capabilities with physical retail experiences have become increasingly important for retailers seeking to capture consumer spending at multiple touchpoints.

### 3.3 Categories Most Affected by E-Commerce

The impact of e-commerce has not been uniform across all retail categories. Certain sectors have experienced more dramatic disruption than others, reflecting differences in the suitability of products for online sales, the established strength of physical retail in particular categories, and the relative ease or difficulty of transitioning to digital channels.

Clothing and fashion represent the most significant e-commerce category in South Africa, accounting for 30.2% of online purchases[13]. The tactile nature of clothing, the importance of fit and feel, and the historical dominance of physical stores in fashion retail had initially suggested that this category might be relatively resistant to online disruption. However, the convenience of home delivery, the ability to try on items at home with easy return policies, and the wider selection available online have proven compelling for South African consumers. The entry of international fast-fashion platforms like Shein and Temu has further accelerated this trend, with these platforms capturing approximately 40% of online clothing sales in 2024[14].

Groceries and fast-moving consumer goods represent the category with the most visible and transformative growth in e-commerce. The sector has experienced double-digit growth, driven primarily by the proliferation of on-demand delivery applications from major supermarket chains[1][11]. Checkers Sixty60, Woolworths' Woolies Dash, and Pick n Pay's delivery services have fundamentally altered the grocery shopping experience for many urban South Africans. The grocery category accounted for 18.4% of online purchases in 2024, with the trajectory strongly upward[13].

Electronics and technology products constitute another highly affected category. The complexity and variety of consumer electronics make them well-suited to online research and purchase, while the growth of online marketplaces has made it easier to compare specifications and prices across multiple brands. Computer hardware and accessories accounted for 16.6% of online purchases, while electronic goods represented 13.7%[13].

Health and beauty products, gifts, and footwear also feature prominently in the e-commerce mix, collectively demonstrating the broad-based nature of the online shopping shift across retail categories.

### 3.4 Geographic Patterns: Urban Versus Rural Adoption

The adoption of e-commerce in South Africa exhibits significant geographic variation, reflecting the country's uneven development patterns, infrastructure constraints, and demographic characteristics. The digital divide between urban and rural communities remains pronounced, with important implications for the future evolution of the market.

Research indicates that online shopping participation in South Africa's largest urban areas (populations of 250,000 or more) reaches 39.6%, compared to only 24.8% in non-metro and rural areas[15][16]. This urban-rural gap of approximately 15 percentage points represents a significant structural disparity in market access. The gap has actually widened in recent years, with urban participation growing by 3.9 percentage points between 2023 and 2024 while non-metro participation declined by 2.6 percentage points over the same period[16].

At the provincial level, Gauteng leads with 45.4% online shopping participation, reflecting its status as South Africa's economic hub and its highly urbanized population[15]. The Western Cape follows at 36.7%, having experienced strong growth of 9.1 percentage points between 2023 and 2024[15]. KwaZulu-Natal recorded 32.8% participation, though this represented a decline of 5.5 percentage points from the previous year. The remaining provinces show progressively lower participation rates, with the Eastern Cape at 18.5% representing the lowest level among provinces with meaningful sample sizes.

Within metropolitan areas, Pretoria leads with 45.0% online shopping participation, followed by Johannesburg at 39.3%[17]. The gap between the highest-performing metro (Pretoria at 45.0%) and the lowest-performing major metro (Cape Town at 26.3%) narrowed dramatically from 38.6 percentage points in 2023 to just 18.7 percentage points in 2024[17]. This convergence suggests that e-commerce access is becoming more evenly distributed across South Africa's major urban centers, though significant disparities remain.

The township economy, which represents a significant component of South Africa's retail landscape, shows mixed e-commerce adoption patterns. Weekly online shopping in townships stands at approximately 21.9%, slightly below the national average but reflecting the growing importance of mobile-based access to online platforms[18]. The township economy, estimated at nearly R900 billion in annual value and hosting over 1.1 million micro, small, and medium enterprises, represents both a market opportunity and a challenge for e-commerce expansion[6].

## 4. Traditional Retail Impact Assessment

### 4.1 In-Store Sales Performance and Market Share Erosion

The rapid growth of e-commerce has begun to exert measurable pressure on traditional in-store retail sales, though the overall impact remains uneven across retail formats and categories. The structural gap between online and offline growth rates, which currently stands at more than ten times, has created a dynamic whereby e-commerce is capturing an increasing share of a slowly growing or stagnant total retail market rather than merely diverting sales from physical stores[3][8].

Physical retail growth has remained sluggish, reflecting the challenging macroeconomic environment, persistent load-shedding, high unemployment, and constrained consumer spending power. Overall retail trade growth reached just 2.5% in 2024, with offline retail growth of only 1.6% by June 2025[1][3]. These figures contrast sharply with e-commerce growth rates, creating an inherent market share transfer dynamic that favors digital channels.

The impact on traditional retailers has varied significantly by sector and format. Fashion and clothing retailers have experienced the most significant disruption, with the combined forces of international online platforms and domestic e-commerce competitors eroding the customer base of established players. Department stores and specialty retailers focusing on electronics, home goods, and apparel have similarly reported competitive pressures. Grocery retailers have been more resilient, as the买菜 (daily grocery shopping) habit remains deeply ingrained and the challenges of fresh food delivery have provided some protection to physical supermarkets. However, even grocery retail is experiencing structural change, with supermarket chains reporting growing volumes through their digital channels while in-store traffic has moderated.

The preference data provides further evidence of shifting consumer behavior. The proportion of South African consumers who express a preference for in-store shopping fell from 28.5% in 2023 to 21.9% in 2024, representing a decline of 6.6 percentage points within a single year[12]. This sharp reduction in in-store preference, combined with the growing acceptance and adoption of online shopping, suggests that the traditional retail sector faces an accelerating structural challenge rather than a temporary disruption.

### 4.2 Store Closures and Retail Space Rationalization

While comprehensive data on store closure rates specifically attributable to e-commerce competition is limited, various indicators suggest that traditional retail space is under pressure in South Africa. The broader retail sector has experienced challenges including economic headwinds, load-shedding costs, and changing consumer patterns that have contributed to a rationalization of physical retail footprints.

Statistics South Africa reported that 1,534 businesses closed in South Africa in 2025, down 1.1% from 1,551 closures recorded in 2024[19]. While these figures encompass business closures across all sectors and do not isolate the specific impact of e-commerce, they provide context for the challenging operating environment facing traditional retailers. The retail sector has been particularly affected by structural shifts in consumer spending patterns and the rising cost of doing business.

Shopping mall operators have acknowledged the competitive pressure from e-commerce, though the sector has shown resilience particularly for grocery-anchored centers that benefit from regular foot traffic. Reports indicate that traditional mall setups are experiencing declining foot traffic in some areas, prompting landlords to rethink tenant mixes and consider more experiential offerings that cannot be replicated online[20]. The transformation of retail space toward service-oriented and experience-based tenants represents an adaptation strategy rather than a retreat, but it also signals the recognition that pure commodity retailing is increasingly difficult to sustain against digital competition.

The COVID-19 pandemic had initially been expected to trigger a wave of retail insolvencies and store closures, and some of these closures have indeed occurred. However, the survival of many traditional retailers through the pandemic period and their subsequent adaptation to omnichannel strategies has demonstrated the continued relevance of physical retail, at least in the medium term. The long-term trajectory remains uncertain, particularly as e-commerce penetration continues to climb.

### 4.3 Profit Margin Compression

Traditional retailers in South Africa face margin pressure from multiple directions, of which e-commerce competition is one significant factor. The competitive intensity introduced by online platforms has increased price transparency and facilitated comparison shopping, making it more difficult for retailers with physical footprints to maintain historical margin levels.

The e-commerce operation itself poses profitability challenges for traditional retailers. The costs of last-mile delivery, returns handling, digital platform maintenance, and customer acquisition in the online channel are substantial and can erode or eliminate the margin advantages of physical retail. Research indicates that cart abandonment due to shipping fees has become the primary driver of online purchase non-completion, cited by 31.3% of respondents in 2025, up from 13.9% in 2024[21]. This finding underscores the sensitivity of South African consumers to delivery costs and the difficulty of achieving profitable delivery-based sales.

However, traditional retailers have demonstrated some resilience in their e-commerce profitability. The World Wide Worx survey found that 74% of online retailers were currently profitable, with 42% describing their operations as "very profitable"[22]. This suggests that the integration of e-commerce with established retail operations can yield positive returns when executed effectively, though it also highlights the risk that underperforming digital operations can become a drag on overall business profitability.

The competitive pressure from international platforms, particularly Shein and Temu, has introduced additional margin complexity. These platforms benefit from lower cost structures, including minimal physical presence requirements, optimized global supply chains, and favorable customs treatment that local retailers cannot easily replicate. The resulting price competition has forced domestic retailers to evaluate their positioning strategies, with some choosing to compete on price while others differentiate through quality, service, or local relevance.

## 5. Competitive Dynamics and Market Response

### 5.1 Traditional Retailers' E-Commerce Initiatives

South Africa's established retail groups have mounted significant responses to the e-commerce challenge, with varying degrees of success and strategic clarity. The intensity of competition in the online grocery segment illustrates the scale of these efforts and their impact on the broader market structure.

Shoprite, Africa's largest retailer by revenue, has emerged as an e-commerce leader through its Checkers Sixty60 platform. The service achieved remarkable growth of 47% in the first half of 2025, generating nearly R19 billion in sales[1][11]. The success of Sixty60 has been attributed to its focus on speed and reliability, with delivery time slots that appeal to time-pressed urban consumers. Shoprite's operational scale, extensive store network, and strong logistics capabilities have supported the platform's growth, and the company has estimated that it holds approximately 80% of South Africa's grocery delivery market[11].

Pick n Pay, once considered a potential consolidator in the South African grocery sector, has pursued an e-commerce strategy that has shown strong growth but has struggled to match the scale of competitors. The retailer reported online sales growth of 60.6% in fiscal year 2024 for its on-demand and scheduled delivery services[8]. However, Pick n Pay has faced intense competition and has seen its market position challenged both in physical retail and online. The company has been working to revitalize its digital offering following a period of strategic uncertainty, though its e-commerce market share remains below that of Checkers Sixty60.

Woolworths has adopted a differentiated positioning in the e-commerce landscape, emphasizing quality, style, and customer experience rather than competing primarily on price. The group's Fashion, Beauty, and Home online sales grew by 37.2% in the first half of fiscal 2025, while its Woolies Dash grocery delivery service achieved growth of 49.2%[1][8]. Woolworths' strength lies in its affluent customer base, strong brand recognition, and curated product assortment that appeals to consumers seeking premium products. The group has invested in its digital platform capabilities and omnichannel integration, enabling features such as click-and-collect and returns across channels.

The Foschini Group (TFG), a diversified retail conglomerate, has pursued e-commerce growth through its Bash platform, which achieved 40.2% growth in the first quarter of fiscal 2026 and contributed 12% of group sales[1][8]. TFG's multi-brand strategy has been adapted for the digital environment, with the platform offering products across the group's various retail concepts. Similarly, Truworths reported online sales growth of 38% in late 2024, with online sales now representing 6% of the company's South African sales[8].

### 5.2 Takealot's Market Dominance

The South African e-commerce landscape is characterized by the overwhelming dominance of Takealot, which has established itself as the country's leading online marketplace by a substantial margin. Takealot commands 31.9% of the online retail market, more than double the share of its nearest competitor[14][23]. This concentration of market share reflects years of investment, strategic acquisitions, and operational excellence that have created significant barriers to entry for competitors.

Takealot's dominance has been reinforced by its comprehensive ecosystem approach. The platform has invested heavily in logistics infrastructure, including warehouse facilities, delivery fleet, and technology systems that enable efficient order fulfillment and customer service. The acquisition of Mr Delivery in 2014 provided last-mile delivery capabilities that have been integrated into the Takealot offering[6]. The platform has also expanded from its origins in books and electronics to general merchandise, offering a wide product assortment that competes across multiple categories.

Despite its market leadership, Takealot has faced intensifying competitive pressure from multiple directions. Amazon's entry in May 2024 represented the most significant competitive challenge in the platform's history, bringing substantial resources and global expertise to the South African market[24]. While Amazon launched without its Prime membership program and with a more limited initial product assortment than some had anticipated, its entry signaled the recognition of South Africa's e-commerce potential by the world's leading online retailer.

The competitive dynamics between Takealot and Amazon illustrate the tension between local market knowledge and global scale advantages. Takealot benefits from its deep understanding of South African consumer preferences, its established seller relationships, and its integrated logistics network. Amazon brings global best practices, technological capabilities, and the credibility of a world-renowned brand. The outcome of this competition remains uncertain, though early indicators suggest that Takealot has maintained its market leadership position[25].

### 5.3 Amazon's Entry and Market Impact

Amazon's launch of its South African marketplace in May 2024 marked a watershed moment in the development of the country's e-commerce sector. The American e-commerce giant's entry, following its announcement in October 2023, represented the most significant competitive development in the market since Takealot's emergence[24][26].

Amazon.co.za launched with approximately 150,000 products across 15 categories, representing a relatively modest initial catalog compared to the company's offerings in more mature markets[10]. The platform initially focused on categories including electronics, home, fashion, and beauty, with expansion into groceries, pet food, and health supplements by early 2025. Notably, Amazon launched without its Prime membership program, meaning South African customers did not have access to the free expedited shipping and streaming services that have driven Prime adoption in other markets.

The strategic rationale for Amazon's South African entry reflects the country's position as the largest economy in Africa and the recognition of untapped e-commerce potential. More than 60% of sales on Amazon's platform globally come from independent third-party sellers, and the company has been working to develop a seller ecosystem in South Africa. In January 2025, Amazon opened a Walk-in seller centre in Cape Town to support local businesses in accessing the platform[10].

Amazon's market share has grown steadily since launch, reaching 12.3% of online retail by late 2024[14][23]. While this represents significant penetration for a new entrant, it also illustrates the challenge of displacing entrenched local players. Amazon's performance has been hampered by the absence of Prime, limited product selection relative to expectations, and the strong loyalty of South African consumers to domestic platforms.

### 5.4 International Platforms: Shein and Temu

The emergence of Chinese fast-fashion platforms Shein and Temu has introduced a new competitive dynamic in South Africa's e-commerce market, with particular intensity in the clothing and fashion segment. These platforms have demonstrated rapid growth trajectories that have alarmed domestic retailers and policymakers alike.

Combined, Shein and Temu achieved estimated sales of R7.3 billion in 2024, capturing approximately 15.3% of the online retail market and approximately 40% of online clothing sales specifically[14][23]. This rapid ascent has been driven by extremely competitive pricing, a vast product assortment that emphasizes trendy items at low price points, and aggressive social media marketing that has resonated with younger consumers.

The competitive threat posed by Shein and Temu extends beyond market share diversion. These platforms benefit from business models that differ fundamentally from traditional retail. They ship directly from manufacturers, primarily in China, using small-package logistics that exploit loopholes in South African customs and VAT regulations. Until regulatory changes in late 2024, goods under R500 could clear customs with only 20% import duty, without the VAT and standard 45% duty that local retailers importing textiles must pay[6]. This regulatory asymmetry created substantial cost advantages for international platforms that have been only partially addressed by recent changes.

The growth of Shein and Temu has raised significant concerns about the impact on domestic manufacturing and retail employment. Research from the Localisation Support Fund suggests that up to 34,000 local manufacturing and retail jobs could be at risk by 2030 if the current trajectory of international platform growth continues unchecked[4][5]. The fashion and textile sector has been particularly affected, with reports linking Shein and Temu sales to over 8,000 lost jobs in South Africa[5].

Regulatory responses have begun to address these concerns. From September to November 2024, South Africa's revenue authority implemented changes including the application of VAT to all low-value parcels and the replacement of flat 20% duty rates with full WCO tariff rates[10]. Additional tightening of declaration rules took effect in April 2025. These changes are expected to slow the growth of international platforms, though their full impact remains to be seen.

## 6. Payment Methods and Financial Infrastructure

### 6.1 Evolution of Payment Preferences

The evolution of payment methods in South Africa's e-commerce sector reflects both the maturation of digital payments infrastructure and changing consumer preferences. The payment landscape has become increasingly diverse, with traditional card-based payments remaining dominant while alternative methods gain traction.

Cards, encompassing both debit and credit variants, remain the primary payment method for online purchases in South Africa, with approximately 50% of consumers preferring card payments for e-commerce transactions[23]. Debit cards are particularly prominent given their accessibility to the broader population, while credit cards serve as the primary payment method for subscription services such as streaming platforms. The strong position of cards reflects their acceptance across virtually all e-commerce platforms, their integration with bank security systems, and the familiarity of South African consumers with card-based transactions.

Electronic Funds Transfer (EFT) represents the second major payment rail, with approximately 26% of consumers preferring this method for online purchases[23]. EFT payments have grown in popularity due to their安全性 (security) and the instant nature of modern instant EFT solutions. The adoption of real-time payment systems such as PayShap has facilitated faster EFT transactions and reduced friction in the payment process. Among surveyed retailers, 94.5% reported accepting EFT payments, making it the most widely accepted payment method after debit cards[22].

Buy Now, Pay Later (BNPL) services have emerged as a significant growth category, though from a relatively small base. BNPL adoption doubled between 2024 and 2025, with the payment method's market share increasing from 3% to 6%[27]. BNPL has become particularly popular for big-ticket purchases and higher-margin items, with fashion and electronics merchants reporting 60% higher average order values when BNPL is selected[9]. The growth of BNPL reflects the broader appeal of payment flexibility, particularly among younger consumers and those seeking to manage cash flow.

Mobile wallets and emerging payment methods have gained visibility but remain minority layers in the overall payment mix. Capitec Pay, South Africa's largest homegrown mobile payment solution, has achieved notable adoption, while Apple Pay and Google Wallet have seen gradual uptake. Approximately 7% of consumers prefer mobile wallets for online purchases[23].

### 6.2 BNPL Growth and Market Dynamics

The BNPL sector in South Africa has experienced explosive growth, driven by changing consumer attitudes toward credit, the entry of specialized providers, and the integration of BNPL options by major e-commerce platforms. This growth represents a significant evolution in how South Africans finance their purchases.

The doubling of BNPL market share from 3% in 2024 to 6% in 2025 signals growing acceptance of the payment method among both merchants and consumers[27]. Payfast's "State of Pay 2025" study found that 34% of consumers had requested merchants to implement BNPL options, indicating strong demand-side pull. Among respondents, 32% reported usage of credit facilities like Mobicred, which operates a network of over 45,000 merchants[27].

The demographic profile of BNPL users reveals interesting patterns. BNPL adoption is particularly concentrated among younger consumers and those purchasing higher-value items. Fashion and electronics merchants report that BNPL selection correlates with significantly higher average transaction values, suggesting that the payment method enables consumers to make purchases they might otherwise defer[9]. This demand-stimulation effect has made BNPL an attractive option for retailers seeking to increase conversion rates and order values.

The BNPL market in South Africa is projected to grow substantially in the coming years. Market research indicates that the South Africa BNPL market generated revenue of USD 104.0 million in 2024 and is expected to reach USD 913.6 million by 2033, representing a compound annual growth rate of approximately 27%[28]. The Africa-wide BNPL market is forecast to expand from USD 5.2 billion in 2025 to USD 16.8 billion by 2031[29].

### 6.3 Cash on Delivery and Alternative Methods

Despite the growth of digital payments, cash on delivery (COD) remains relevant in South Africa's e-commerce landscape, reflecting the preferences of certain consumer segments and the practical requirements of reaching underbanked populations. Research indicates that 37.3% of e-commerce retailers offered COD as a payment option in 2025, up significantly from 11.9% in 2024[21].

The persistence of COD relates to several factors. A significant proportion of South Africans remain outside the formal banking system or have limited access to card-based payment instruments. Trust concerns, particularly among newer online shoppers, may make the ability to pay upon receipt preferable to prepayment. Additionally, the cultural habit of paying upon delivery for certain product categories has translated into online purchasing expectations.

The offering of collection and return points has emerged as an important complement to delivery-based e-commerce, with 35.8% of retailers offering this option in 2025[21]. Collection points provide flexibility for consumers who may not be available to receive deliveries, while also reducing the cost and complexity of last-mile logistics. The growth of click-and-collect options reflects the integration of online and offline retail channels.

## 7. Market Structure Changes and Industry Evolution

### 7.1 Consolidation in Traditional Retail

The South African traditional retail sector has experienced significant consolidation pressure, driven by e-commerce competition, economic headwinds, and strategic imperatives to achieve scale in both physical and digital channels. While consolidation is not new to South African retail, the e-commerce dimension has added urgency and complexity to these dynamics.

The proposed acquisition of certain Pick n Pay assets by Shoprite represents the most significant consolidation development in South African grocery retail in years. The transaction, which has received regulatory attention, would see Shoprite acquire 56 supermarkets and 43 liquor stores from Pick n Pay[30]. The rationale for the acquisition reflects Shoprite's strategy of expanding its footprint and achieving operational synergies, while Pick n Pay has been working to rationalize its portfolio and focus on its core strengths. The consolidation would further strengthen Shoprite's dominant market position and its ability to invest in e-commerce capabilities.

Pick n Pay's strategic challenges have been accumulating over an extended period. The retailer has faced intense competition from Shoprite in the value segment, from Woolworths in the premium segment, and from the rapid growth of convenience and discount formats. The company's attempts to transform its operations and re-establish competitive positioning have yielded mixed results, and its e-commerce strategy, while showing growth, has not achieved the scale necessary to offset pressures in the physical channel.

Woolworths has maintained its positioning as South Africa's most upscale grocery retailer, though it too faces competitive pressures. The group's "Differentiated Food" strategy has emphasized quality, innovation, and the exclusive brands that characterize its product assortment. However, the competitive intensity in the grocery sector has compressed margins and forced increased investment in price competitiveness and digital capabilities.

### 7.2 New Market Entrants and Platform Competition

The South African e-commerce landscape has seen significant new entry activity, reflecting both the opportunities presented by a growing market and the threat of displacement by international competitors. Beyond Amazon, which represents the most significant new entrant, the market has witnessed the emergence and growth of various specialized and niche platforms.

Bash, the e-commerce platform operated by The Foschini Group, has established itself as a significant player with 12% market share in terms of group sales contribution[8]. TFG's approach has leveraged the company's existing brand portfolio, customer relationships, and retail infrastructure to build an omnichannel capability that serves shoppers across multiple product categories.

Specialized platforms have also proliferated, including fashion-focused retailers, grocery delivery services, and category-specific marketplaces. The competitive intensity among domestic platforms has increased substantially, with players competing not only on price but also on delivery speed, product selection, customer service, and user experience.

The competitive threat from international platforms has catalyzed a broader re-evaluation of South African retail strategy. Domestic retailers have highlighted what they characterize as an unlevel playing field, noting that international platforms benefit from customs duty and tax concessions, operational scale advantages, low compliance costs, and minimal regulatory obligations relative to local businesses with physical presence[6]. The call for regulatory intervention has grown louder, with the 2024 regulatory changes representing a initial response.

### 7.3 Employment Effects and Labor Market Implications

The employment impact of e-commerce growth on South Africa's retail sector represents one of the most consequential and contested dimensions of the industry's transformation. The shift from physical to digital retail channels has significant implications for employment patterns, skill requirements, and income distribution.

Research suggests that up to 34,000 local manufacturing and retail jobs could be at risk by 2030 if the current trajectory of international e-commerce platform growth continues unchecked[4][5]. This projection, from the Localisation Support Fund, has been widely cited in discussions of e-commerce policy. The estimate of 8,100 jobs not materializing by 2024 due to offshore competition, with approximately two-thirds in retail and one-third in manufacturing, provides a more immediate quantification of the impact[12].

The textile and clothing sector has been particularly affected by import competition from platforms like Shein and Temu. The combined market share of these platforms in online clothing reached approximately 40% in 2024, diverting demand from domestic manufacturers and retailers[14]. The competitive pressure has manifested in reduced production volumes, declining employment, and business closures in the local textile industry.

However, the employment equation is not solely negative. E-commerce has created jobs in logistics, delivery services, digital marketing, and technology development. Takealot has recruited approximately 2,500 personal shoppers as of December 2024, with plans to expand this number to 5,000 by 2028[31]. The broader digital economy has generated employment across various segments, including warehouse operations, customer service, and platform management.

The net employment effect remains uncertain and will depend on the balance between job creation in emerging sectors and job displacement in traditional retail and manufacturing. The transition poses challenges for workers who may lack the skills required for digital-era employment, highlighting the importance of reskilling and upskilling initiatives.

## 8. Consumer Behavior Patterns and Preferences

### 8.1 Shopping Frequency and Basket Size Trends

The behavioral patterns of South African online shoppers have evolved substantially, with important implications for the frequency and value of e-commerce transactions. These patterns reflect the maturation of the market and the development of habitual online shopping behaviors.

Weekly online shopping has become increasingly common, particularly among certain demographic segments. Research indicates that 21% of South Africans who shop online do so weekly or more frequently, with 6% shopping more than once a week and 15% shopping once per week[23]. Among the 25-34 age group, weekly shopping rates reach 32.3%, the highest of any demographic cohort[18]. The concentration of frequent shoppers in higher-income brackets and younger age groups reflects the demographic profile of South Africa's most advanced e-commerce consumers.

Basket sizes have shifted decisively upward, indicating that consumers are becoming more comfortable with larger online purchases. The proportion of shoppers spending R2,001 or more in the previous six months grew by 9.2 percentage points between 2023 and 2024, while purchases under R100 declined by 7.9 percentage points over the same period[13][32]. This pattern suggests that online shopping is increasingly being used for substantive household purchases rather than occasional small-item buying.

The composition of baskets has also evolved, with a migration from very small transactions to mid-range and high-value purchases. The R101-R600 range now accounts for approximately 40.7% of last-month spending, reflecting considered purchases across multiple product categories rather than impulse buying of individual items[32]. This shift has positive implications for e-commerce profitability, as higher-value orders can better absorb the fixed costs of fulfillment and delivery.

### 8.2 Delivery Expectations and Satisfaction

Delivery has emerged as a critical battleground for e-commerce competitiveness, with consumer expectations for speed, reliability, and cost becoming increasingly demanding. The ability to meet these expectations has become a key differentiator among platforms and a significant driver of customer satisfaction and loyalty.

Instant and same-day delivery has become an expectation for grocery and essential item purchases in urban areas. Checkers Sixty60 has built its competitive positioning around rapid delivery, while Woolworths and Pick n Pay have invested in their own delivery capabilities to meet this standard. The willingness of consumers to pay for delivery speed varies, with research indicating that 31.3% of abandoned carts cite shipping fees as the primary reason for non-completion[21].

The geographic expansion of e-commerce to smaller towns and middle-income households has introduced delivery challenges that the industry is still addressing. Rural and peri-urban areas often lack the logistics infrastructure to support rapid delivery, and the cost of delivery to these areas can exceed the value of the order itself. Approximately 16% of e-commerce retailers reported offering delivery to townships as a strategic priority in 2025, though this represented a decline from 53.2% in 2024[21].

Consumer satisfaction with delivery services has shown general improvement, though significant challenges remain. The proportion of retailers reporting payment failures as a major challenge declined from 33.9% in 2024 to 9.0% in 2025, while customer query management improved from 33.7% finding this challenging to only 6.0%[33]. These improvements suggest that the industry is maturing in its operational capabilities, though delivery costs and reliability remain pain points.

### 8.3 Trust and Platform Preferences

Consumer trust has become a central dynamic in South Africa's e-commerce market, shaping platform preferences and influencing the competitive balance between local and international players. The trust dimension reveals important insights into how consumers evaluate online shopping options.

South African consumers demonstrate a strong preference for local e-commerce platforms, with approximately 50% reporting that they most frequently use South African platforms for online shopping[23]. Research indicates that 46% of consumers trust domestic sites more than international alternatives, while only 10% express greater trust in international platforms[23]. This preference for local platforms has been a significant factor in Takealot's continued dominance despite Amazon's entry.

The trust advantage of local platforms reflects several factors. Consumers perceive domestic retailers as offering better customer service, more accessible returns processes, and greater accountability. The physical presence of these retailers, with stores that consumers can visit to resolve issues, reinforces trust perceptions. Additionally, familiarity with local brands and the cultural resonance of domestic platforms contribute to preference formation.

International platforms face a more complex trust dynamic. While they are recognized for competitive pricing and product variety, concerns about delivery times, customs fees, and return policies create friction in the purchase decision[34]. The challenges of international platforms are reflected in usage data: 56% of South Africans report never shopping on Shein or Temu, and only 5% make weekly purchases on these platforms[23].

The trust differential has important competitive implications. As long as domestic platforms can maintain their service quality and customer experience advantages, they are likely to retain the majority of local e-commerce spending. However, international platforms may continue to attract price-sensitive consumers and those seeking specific products not available locally.

## 9. Future Outlook and Strategic Implications

### 9.1 Growth Projections and Market Evolution

The trajectory of South Africa's e-commerce sector points toward continued substantial growth, though the rate of expansion is expected to moderate as market penetration increases. The structural drivers of growth—including increasing internet access, smartphone adoption, digital literacy, and lifestyle convenience—remain firmly in place.

Market projections suggest that online retail could reach R150 billion by 2027, representing approximately 12% of total retail turnover[1][6]. The Mordor Intelligence forecast of USD 38.13 billion in 2025 growing to USD 63.06 billion by 2031 indicates compound annual growth of approximately 8.54% over the period[9]. These figures position South Africa as one of the more attractive e-commerce markets globally, though the absolute size remains modest relative to developed market benchmarks.

The composition of e-commerce growth is expected to continue shifting across categories. Grocery and fast-moving consumer goods will likely account for an increasing share of online retail as delivery infrastructure matures and consumer habits solidify. Fashion and electronics will remain important categories, though growth rates may moderate as market saturation increases. The emergence of new categories, including health services, professional services, and B2B e-commerce, could provide additional growth avenues.

### 9.2 Challenges and Opportunities

The development of South Africa's e-commerce sector faces several significant challenges that will shape its evolution and competitive dynamics. Successfully navigating these challenges will determine which market participants thrive and which fall behind.

Infrastructure constraints represent a fundamental challenge. Load-shedding has disrupted retail operations and added costs for both physical stores and distribution centers. Logistics infrastructure, particularly for last-mile delivery in less densely populated areas, requires substantial investment to achieve the service levels that consumers increasingly expect. The digital divide, with 22.5% of South Africans lacking internet access, limits the addressable market for e-commerce[15].

Regulatory evolution will significantly impact the competitive landscape. The changes implemented in late 2024 and 2025 affecting customs duty and VAT on low-value imports represent a significant shift that has yet to fully play out. Further regulatory changes addressing platform competition, consumer protection, and data privacy could reshape the operating environment for e-commerce businesses.

The employment transition represents both a challenge and an opportunity. The displacement of jobs in traditional retail and manufacturing by e-commerce and international platform competition requires coordinated responses in education, retraining, and social support. Simultaneously, the growth of digital economy employment in logistics, technology, and services creates new opportunities for workers with appropriate skills.

The opportunities in South Africa's e-commerce sector are substantial for businesses that can effectively address consumer needs. The underserved nature of significant population segments, including those in townships and rural areas, represents untapped market potential. The growth of mobile-based commerce creates opportunities for platform innovation and service differentiation. The increasing sophistication of South African consumers offers possibilities for premium service offerings and enhanced customer experiences.

## 10. Conclusion

South Africa's e-commerce sector has reached a critical juncture in its development, having evolved from a marginal channel to a structural force in the retail landscape. The growth from less than 1% of total retail in 2019 to approximately 10% in 2025 represents one of the most significant retail transformations in the country's economic history, driven by technological advancement, changing consumer behavior, and strategic responses from both domestic and international market participants.

The impact on traditional retail has been substantial but not catastrophic. While physical retail growth has stalled relative to e-commerce, the overall retail market has demonstrated resilience, and many traditional retailers have adapted successfully through omnichannel strategies and e-commerce investments. The competitive dynamics have intensified dramatically, with established players facing challenges from new entrants, international platforms, and the shifting preferences of increasingly sophisticated consumers.

The employment implications represent the most significant concern arising from the e-commerce transition. The potential loss of up to 34,000 jobs in manufacturing and traditional retail by 2030, if international platform growth continues unchecked, highlights the need for thoughtful policy responses and industry adaptation. However, the net employment effect will depend on the balance between job creation in emerging digital economy segments and displacement in traditional sectors.

Looking ahead, South Africa's e-commerce sector is well-positioned for continued growth, though the rate of expansion will moderate as market penetration increases. The structural drivers of growth remain favorable, and the competitive responses of domestic retailers demonstrate the adaptability of the local industry. The resolution of infrastructure constraints, the evolution of regulatory frameworks, and the management of the employment transition will determine whether the e-commerce transformation delivers broadly shared prosperity or exacerbates existing inequalities.

The South African retail landscape is no longer asking whether e-commerce will transform traditional business models but rather how quickly and comprehensively this transformation will occur. For market participants, the imperative is clear: embrace digital capabilities, differentiate through customer experience, and adapt organizational cultures and operational models to thrive in an increasingly connected and competitive retail environment.
