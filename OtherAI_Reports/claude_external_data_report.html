⚠ Structural Context

South Africa's GDP growth has averaged only 0.76% per year over the last decade — well below population growth (~1.5%), meaning GDP per capita has been declining. The 2023 performance was particularly damaging: the SARB estimated load shedding alone cost 1.8 percentage points of GDP growth. Manufacturing has posted negative growth for two consecutive years (2024–2025). Construction sector is in its 9th consecutive year of decline.

🔴 The Cumulative Damage

While inflation is now low, the cumulative price level increase from 2021–2023 was severe. Prices rose ~18–22% across that window. This means a consumer's basket of goods that cost R100 in 2020 now costs ~R122 in 2025 — even though current inflation is at 3%. The damage is baked in. Real wages were eroded for 2+ years, with BankservAfrica data showing average nominal salaries fell 10.4% in April 2023 alone.

📊 Key Drivers of 2023 Consumer Squeeze

Electricity tariffs increased 18.65% in April 2023. Food prices rose 12%+. Fuel averaged R23+/litre inland. Interest on home loans surged. The combined hit on disposable income was unprecedented in the post-2008 era. Lower-income households (LSM 1–5) experienced the worst real income destruction, as food and energy represent a higher share of their spending basket.

🔴 Impact on Consumer Spending 2023–2024

SA household debt-to-disposable income has been around 63–67%. With prime at 11.75%, a consumer with R300K in personal loans and a R1.5M bond was paying an additional ~R2,400/month versus 2021 levels. This directly suppressed discretionary spending on non-essential retail. Furniture, electronics, clothing, and leisure spend contracted sharply. The "durable goods" segment of the CCI sub-index hit -35 in Q2 2023 — near record lows.

2023 — THE WORST YEAR

South Africa experienced Stage 6 load shedding for extended periods. No single day was load-shedding free. Eskom's Energy Availability Factor (EAF) fell to 50.4%. Businesses spent R1B+ on diesel for generators. Retailers like TFG lost R400M in two weeks; Shoprite spent R93M/month extra on diesel. Small businesses could not afford mitigation — 40% lost 20%+ of revenue. Consumer footfall in physical retail collapsed during outages. Electricity tariff hikes of 18.65% were approved, compounding cost of living.

2024–2025 — THE RECOVERY

From April 2024, load shedding was effectively eliminated. Diesel usage for generators dropped from 12% to 6% of Eskom capacity. Business costs normalised. Consumer footfall recovered. However, electricity tariffs remained elevated (12.74% increase approved for 2024). The psychological damage lingered — consumers remained cautious and price-sensitive even as supply improved. A brief return to Stage 6 in early 2025 rekindled fears and contributed to the CCI crash to -20 in Q1 2025.

⚠ What this means for retail demand

With 1 in 3 South Africans unemployed and youth unemployment near 60%, the effective consumer base is severely constrained. The employed middle class drives most retail spending. When this group faces simultaneous high inflation, high interest rates, AND load shedding — the combination creates a spending contraction that hits all retail segments. Social grants (SRD R350→R370) partially cushion the lowest income segments but do not drive discretionary retail. The GNU formation in mid-2024 improved sentiment but structural unemployment remained unchanged.

Consumer Confidence Index (FNB/BER CCI)

About the CCI

The FNB/BER Consumer Confidence Index measures consumer willingness to spend. Range: -100 to +100. Historical average since 1994: -1. Negative readings indicate consumers are more pessimistic than optimistic about spending. Deeply negative readings (-15 and below) correlate strongly with retail contraction.

🔴 Critical Insight: CCI vs Retail Sales Correlation

Every quarter where CCI was below -15, retail trade sales contracted year-on-year. The CCI in Q1–Q2 2023 (-23/-25) coincided with 5 consecutive months of retail decline. The subsequent recovery to -5 in Q3 2024 triggered a rebound in retail, with 2024 online retail growing 35% YoY. The Q1 2025 crash to -20 (VAT shock) immediately suppressed discretionary purchases — the sub-index for "time to buy durable goods" hit -28. This CCI data directly explains Valueco's performance patterns at a macro level.

⚠ The Fuel–Inflation Linkage

South Africa imports both crude oil and refined petroleum products. Every R1/$ depreciation adds approximately 25–30c/litre to the pump price. In 2023, the rand at R18.5+ combined with elevated Brent crude (~$80–90/bbl) kept fuel costs high. Transport costs a significant 5–8% of lower-income household budgets. The 2024–2025 fuel price relief (estimated R1.50–2.50/litre reduction vs 2023) provided meaningful disposable income recovery — contributing directly to the retail recovery visible in 2025 data (+3.7% YoY).

📱 Key Structural Shift: The Load-Shedding → Online Behaviour Link

Paradoxically, load shedding accelerated online shopping behaviour. Consumers with inverters/solar at home discovered they could shop online even during power cuts. Mobile internet maintained connectivity. Takealot, Checkers Sixty60, and Pick n Pay Online all saw surges. This is a structural, permanent behavioural change. Data tariffs fell 25% since 2023 due to ICASA mid-band spectrum allocation, further enabling mobile commerce. South Africa now has over 50 million internet users (2025).

⚠ The Price-Sensitivity Trap

Shein and Temu have permanently recalibrated consumer price expectations. A consumer who bought a T-shirt for R89 from Shein will not return to a local store to pay R299. This price anchoring effect is particularly severe for Valueco-adjacent categories (home goods, household items, electronics accessories). The "shop like a billionaire" campaign psychology — abundance at ultra-low prices — has reset what consumers consider "value." Even SARS's September 2024 VAT closure of the R500 import loophole has not reversed this shift entirely.

Key Psychological Shifts — Factor Analysis

The Permanent Price Reset
Behavioural Economics
Shein/Temu's ultra-low pricing has permanently reset the consumer's internal price reference. After buying a product at 70% below local retail price, anchoring bias prevents return to "full" local prices. This is not just a trend — it is a structural recalibration of price perception that local retailers cannot easily reverse. Combined with inflation-eroded savings, the 2026 consumer is the most price-sensitive in SA retail history.

Trauma-Informed Frugality
Post-Crisis Psychology
The 2022–2023 economic triple-shock (inflation + rate hikes + load shedding) created lasting behavioural scars. Even as macro conditions improve in 2025–2026, consumers show "trauma-informed frugality" — maintaining savings-first behaviour, delaying big purchases, and shopping for value even when they can theoretically afford more. This is visible in the CCI sub-index for durable goods remaining negative even as headline CCI recovered.

IMPACT SEVERITY
Digital Nativity Acceleration
Channel Behaviour
Load shedding paradoxically created a permanent cohort of online shoppers who discovered the channel out of necessity (shopping on mobile during loadshedding). This cohort does not revert. The 50M+ internet users, 71% mobile commerce share, and R96B online retail market in 2024 represent a fundamentally different distribution landscape. The 2026 consumer compares prices across Takealot, Amazon, Shein, Temu, and physical stores before any purchase decision.

Segment Bifurcation
Income Stratification
The 2026 consumer market is bifurcated: high-income households (earning R40K+/month) are recovering — two-pot pension withdrawals of R40B in 2024 created a spending surge, and rate cuts improved their mortgage costs. Meanwhile, lower-income households face persistent unemployment, eroded savings, and reduced two-pot withdrawals in 2025. The FNB/BER CCI Q1 2026 shows high-income confidence recovering to highest since Q4 2024, while low-income confidence deteriorated further.
7/10
IMPACT SEVERITY
Trust Fragmentation
Brand Dynamics
The explosion of choice (Takealot, Amazon, Temu, Shein, Bash, Sixty60) has fragmented brand loyalty. The 2026 SA consumer is omnichannel by default — they compare, they wait, they optimise. 27% of SA online shoppers "compare prices across sites and are willing to wait for lower prices" (Vishakha, 2024). The concept of a loyal repeat customer to any single retailer has fundamentally weakened. This is a structural threat to any physical-first retailer's repeat purchase model.

External Data Verdicts — Confirmed Factors for Sales Decline

Verdict 1: The 2023 Crisis Was A Perfect Storm
The external data confirms that 2023 was objectively the most hostile retail environment since 2020 (COVID). CCI at -25 (2nd worst since 1994). Load shedding cost the economy R2.8T and retailers hundreds of millions directly in diesel, food waste, and lost footfall. Inflation at 6%+. Interest rates at 14-year highs. Any retail business that maintained even flat sales in 2023 was outperforming the macro environment.
Verdict 2: E-Commerce is a Structural Redistributor, Not a Cycle
Online retail grew 29% in 2023 and 35% in 2024 — while physical retail contracted. The channel shift is permanent and accelerating. South Africa will exceed 10% online penetration of total retail in 2025. Businesses without a credible digital channel face structural volume erosion regardless of macro recovery. Shein and Temu alone hold 37% of the online clothing market — this cannot be undone by price matching alone.
Verdict 3: 2025–2026 Is a Recovery Period, But With Structural Scars
The macro environment has genuinely improved: inflation at 3%, repo at 6.75%, power stable, GNU sentiment positive, retail sales +3.7% in 2025. However, consumer psychology carries lasting damage — price sensitivity is elevated, digital comparison-shopping is default behaviour, brand loyalty has collapsed, and the income bifurcation means recovery is uneven. Businesses need different strategies for LSM 5–7 (value-seeking, price-anchored) vs LSM 8–10 (quality/convenience-seeking, recovering confidence).
Verdict 4: The Global Competitive Reset Is Permanent
Amazon, Shein, Temu, and Bash are not temporary disruptions. They represent permanent structural competition with fundamentally different cost structures (global scale, logistics advantages, algorithmic pricing). Local retailers must compete on trust, speed, service, and local knowledge — not on price. SARS's regulatory interventions (VAT on sub-R500 imports) partially level the playing field but the consumer psychology shift is already done. The price anchor has moved.
